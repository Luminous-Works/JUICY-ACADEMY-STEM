# WOMEN IN STEM
## JUICY Academy — Dedicated Module

**Module code:** WIS  
**Level:** Grade 9–University  
**Prerequisites:** None. This module stands alone.  
**Statement:** These women advanced human civilisation. Most curricula do not teach their names.  
That is a choice. This module makes a different one.

---

### Why This Module Exists

Jocelyn Bell Burnell discovered pulsars. Her supervisor received the Nobel Prize.  
Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography revealed the structure of DNA. Watson and Crick received the Nobel Prize.  
Katherine Johnson calculated the trajectory that put John Glenn in orbit. The computer got the credit — until a film was made about her 55 years later.  
Marie Curie won two Nobel Prizes in two separate scientific disciplines and died from the radiation she worked with because nobody told her it was dangerous.  
Dr. Patricia Bath — daughter of a Trinidadian father and African American mother — invented the laser procedure that restored sight to millions of blind people. Her 1988 patent made her the first Black female doctor to receive a medical patent in the United States.

These are not footnotes. These are the main story. The textbooks got it wrong.

---

### The Caribbean and Black Women Question

The history of science has erased Black women, Caribbean women, and women of the Global South with systematic thoroughness. This is not accidental. It is the result of institutional structures that excluded them from laboratories, denied them authorship, denied them credit, denied them prizes, and denied them the professorships from which scientific legacies are built and remembered.

Understanding this is not a political statement. It is an accurate reading of the historical record.

The students in JUICY Academy — many of them Jamaican, Caribbean, of African descent — deserve to see themselves in the history of science. Not as aspirational tokens. As the actual, documented, verifiable record of what Black and Caribbean women have built and discovered.

### A Note on Teaching This Module

These lessons are rigorous science. They are also history. They are also an argument — that the shape of what we call "scientific achievement" is partly determined by who gets credit, and that this shape has been consistently distorted by race and gender.

Students may push back. Encourage it. The facts in these lessons are documented and sourced. The argument is: look at the evidence and decide what it means.

That is, by definition, scientific thinking.
