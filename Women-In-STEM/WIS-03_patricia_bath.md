### Opening Statement

> *"The ability to restore sight is the ultimate gift."*
> — Dr. Patricia Bath

On May 17, 1988, the United States Patent and Trademark Office issued patent number 4,744,360 to Dr. Patricia Era Bath for the **Laserphaco Probe** — a laser device for the surgical removal of cataracts.

With that patent, Dr. Patricia Bath became the first Black female doctor to receive a medical patent in the United States.

She was not the first to invent a laser cataract treatment. She was the first to make it work.

The distinction matters. Her Laserphaco Probe used a precisely calibrated laser — 170–180 mJ/pulse at a specific wavelength — to vaporise the hardened lens of a cataract and replace it with a synthetic lens, restoring clear vision to people who had been blind for decades. She tested the device in Europe, where research facilities had the necessary equipment. She performed the procedure on patients who had been blind for more than 30 years.

They saw.

Dr. Bath never stopped inventing. She held five patents in total. She filed her last patent at age 72.

---

### 1. Who She Was

Dr. Patricia Bath was born on November 4, 1942, in Harlem, New York City.

Her father, **Rupert Bath**, was from Trinidad. He was one of the first Black men to work as a motorman for the New York City subway, and a merchant seaman before that. He read widely, travelled the world, and told his daughter: *"You can go anywhere. See everything. Learn anything."*

Her mother, Gladys, worked as a domestic worker and used her savings to buy Patricia a chemistry set and a microscope.

By age 16, Patricia Bath had won a scholarship to a National Science Foundation summer programme and published research findings that impressed her mentors enough that one of them — a cancer researcher — included her work in a scientific paper. She was in high school.

She graduated high school in two years instead of three.

She attended Hunter College (Bachelor of Arts, 1964), then Howard University College of Medicine (MD, 1968) — graduating with honours. Her internship was at Harlem Hospital. Her residency in ophthalmology was at New York University — she became the first woman to complete that residency programme.

She later became the first woman and first Black person on the faculty of the UCLA Jules Stein Eye Institute.

---

### 2. Community Ophthalmology — A Discovery Made by Looking

Before she invented anything, Dr. Bath made a scientific discovery that changed public health policy.

During her medical training at Harlem Hospital and Columbia University, she noticed something in the data: **Black patients had significantly higher rates of blindness and vision impairment than white patients** at the same institutions.

This was not in the literature. Nobody had formally studied it. Bath designed a research protocol, collected the data, analysed it, and published the finding: rates of blindness in Black Americans were double those of white Americans. The primary cause was lack of access to routine eye care.

She proposed **Community Ophthalmology** — a public health model that brought eye care directly to communities, including nursing homes and schools, rather than waiting for patients to seek care. This model is now standard practice in preventive eye health globally.

She made this discovery not with a microscope or a spectrometer. She made it by paying attention to who was and was not getting treated, and then counting.

### 3. The Physics of the Laserphaco Probe

A **cataract** is the clouding of the eye's natural lens — a transparent, flexible structure that focuses light onto the retina. The lens is primarily composed of water and protein (crystallins). When these proteins denature (lose their normal structure), they aggregate and scatter light, causing the characteristic opacity.

Traditional cataract surgery in the 1980s used an ultrasonic probe to **mechanically fragment** the lens (phacoemulsification) and vacuum out the pieces. The procedure was effective but required a larger incision, longer recovery, and more trauma to surrounding tissue.

Dr. Bath's insight: use laser light — specifically a pulsed Nd:YAG laser — to **vaporise** the cataract instead of fragmenting it mechanically.

**The fundamental physics:**

When a pulsed laser delivers energy to tissue, it can produce three effects depending on pulse duration, energy, and wavelength:
- **Photocoagulation:** tissue heating and protein denaturation (continuous or long-pulse laser)
- **Photodisruption:** plasma formation and acoustic shock wave (very short pulse, very high peak power)
- **Photoablation:** direct molecular bond breaking and tissue ejection (UV wavelengths, short pulse)

Bath's Laserphaco Probe used the photodisruption mechanism. A pulse duration in the nanosecond range creates a plasma at the focal point that expands and collapses, generating a pressure wave that fragments and ablates the cataract material with minimal thermal damage to surrounding tissue.

**Energy delivery:**

$$E_{pulse} = P_{peak} \cdot t_{pulse}$$

For a 170 mJ pulse at nanosecond duration, the peak power is approximately:

$$P_{peak} = \frac{0.170}{10 \times 10^{-9}} = 1.7 \times 10^7 \text{ W} = 17 \text{ megawatts}$$

17 megawatts delivered for 10 nanoseconds, focused to a spot diameter of approximately 50 micrometres. The spatial energy density (irradiance) at that spot:

$$I = \frac{P_{peak}}{A} = \frac{1.7 \times 10^7}{\pi (25 \times 10^{-6})^2} \approx 8.7 \times 10^{15} \text{ W/m}^2$$

At that irradiance, matter does not heat — it ionises instantly. The cataract is not cut. It is converted to plasma and removed.

This was Dr. Bath's instrument. She designed the probe geometry, the pulse parameters, the irrigation/aspiration system to remove the ablated material, and the delivery mechanism for the replacement lens — all in a single minimally invasive instrument.

### 5. What Was Denied — and What Was Not

Dr. Bath applied for a position at the New York Eye and Ear Infirmary during her training. She was turned down. The stated reason, according to her own account: she was both a woman and Black.

She went to Harlem Hospital. She did her research there. She produced the community ophthalmology findings that changed public health policy. Then she went to UCLA and became the first woman, first Black person on their ophthalmology faculty.

She said this about her career: *"I had to fight every step of the way. I didn't always win. But I never stopped."*

What was denied: easy entry. The positions that should have been available to her on merit alone.

What was not denied: the inventions. The five patents. The patients who saw again.

Institutions can deny positions. They cannot deny what a person builds in the time they have.

### Discussion Questions

1. Dr. Bath's community ophthalmology research used a simple comparative method: she counted rates of blindness in different populations served by the same institutions. She was not using specialised equipment or novel statistical methods — she was paying careful attention to data that already existed. What does this suggest about where scientific discoveries can come from? What data around you, right now, might contain an important finding if someone paid attention to it?

2. Calculate the irradiance (W/m²) at the focal spot of a laser with the following parameters: pulse energy = 200 mJ, pulse duration = 5 nanoseconds, focal spot diameter = 60 micrometres. Compare to Dr. Bath's values. Would this be more or less ablative?

3. Dr. Bath grew up in Harlem with a Trinidadian father who read everything, travelled everywhere, and told her the world was open to her. How did her home environment shape her scientific career? What does this suggest about the role of family and community in producing scientists?

4. Dr. Bath's first cataract restoration using the Laserphaco Probe was performed on a patient who had been blind for 30 years. That patient saw again. Write a brief reflection: what is the relationship between scientific invention and human dignity? What does it mean to give someone their sight back?
