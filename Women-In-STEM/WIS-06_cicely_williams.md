# WIS-06: The Name That Fed the World
## Women in STEM — Lesson 6

**Core concept:** Nutritional biochemistry, clinical diagnosis, public health medicine  
**Duration:** 90 minutes  
**Subject:** Dr. Cicely Delphin Williams CBE  
**Born:** Kew Park, St. Andrew, Jamaica, 2 December 1893  
**Died:** 13 July 1992, Oxford, England — aged 98  
**Publication of record:** *The Lancet*, 1935 — "Kwashiorkor: A Nutritional Disease of Children Associated with a Maize Diet"

---

### Opening Statement

> *"I found these children and I didn't know what was wrong with them. The local name was kwashiorkor — 'the sickness the older child gets when the new baby is born.' I described what I saw. That is all a doctor can do."*
> — Dr. Cicely Williams

In the early 1930s, children in the Gold Coast — the country now called Ghana — were dying of a disease that the colonial medical establishment could not name.

They had swollen bellies. Reddened, thinned hair. Skin that peeled in patches. They were lethargic. They grew slowly. They died in numbers that were accepted, at the time, as simply the way things were in tropical Africa.

Colonial medical officers were diagnosing the condition as pellagra — a niacin (Vitamin B3) deficiency — and treating it accordingly. The treatment did not work because the diagnosis was wrong. The children continued to die.

A Jamaican physician named Cicely Williams arrived in the Gold Coast in 1930 as one of the first women in the British Colonial Medical Service. She looked at the children. She talked to the local people. She learned the Ga name for the condition: **kwashiorkor** — "the disease the deposed child gets," the sickness that strikes the first child when a second is born and the mother's breast milk — and its nutrition — is redirected.

She understood immediately that this was not pellagra. The clinical picture was different. The pattern of incidence was different. The local knowledge pointed to something completely specific.

She published her observations in 1933. She published the definitive analysis in *The Lancet* in 1935. She named the condition. She described it. She identified its cause: **protein deficiency** in children transitioning from breast milk to a maize-based diet.

That 1935 paper began the field of protein-energy malnutrition research. The children who did not die in the decades after it was published — across West Africa, across the tropics, across the developing world — are part of her legacy.

There are millions of them.

---

### 1. Who She Was

Cicely Delphin Williams was born in 1893 at Kew Park, a family estate in St. Andrew, Jamaica. Her father, Rowland Williams, was the superintendent of the Mico Training College in Kingston — the oldest teacher training institution in Jamaica, founded in 1834 to educate formerly enslaved people.

She was educated partly in Jamaica, partly in England. She studied at Somerville College, Oxford — one of the first women's colleges at Oxford — and then at King's College Hospital in London, where she completed her medical degree.

She graduated at a time when women doctors in Britain and the colonies were not expected to practice medicine in the serious sense. They were expected to work in maternal and child health — which Cicely Williams did, but not in the way the institution had in mind.

In 1929, she joined the British Colonial Medical Service. She was posted to the Gold Coast in 1930.

---

### 2. The Disease Nobody Named

**Kwashiorkor** is a severe form of protein-energy malnutrition. Its distinguishing features:

| Symptom | Biochemical cause |
|---------|------------------|
| Oedema (fluid swelling, especially abdomen and legs) | Hypoalbuminaemia — insufficient serum albumin to maintain oncotic pressure |
| Hair depigmentation (flag sign) | Reduced cysteine and methionine — sulphur-containing amino acids |
| Skin lesions (dermatosis) | Deficiency of essential amino acids needed for skin cell renewal |
| Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver) | Fatty liver due to impaired lipoprotein synthesis |
| Growth failure | Insufficient amino acids for protein synthesis and tissue growth |
| Anaemia | Iron, folate, and protein deficiency combined |

The biochemistry is now well understood. In 1930, it was not.

The critical distinction Williams made — and that the colonial medical establishment had missed — was between **pellagra** (niacin deficiency, caused by a maize diet lacking tryptophan) and **kwashiorkor** (protein deficiency, caused by a maize diet lacking adequate protein altogether).

Both conditions were associated with maize-based diets in children. Both involved skin changes and growth failure. But the clinical pictures were different, the population distributions were different, and — crucially — the treatments were different. Treating kwashiorkor with niacin would not work. Children would continue to die.

Williams insisted on the distinction. She was largely ignored at first. Senior medical officers in the colonial service maintained the pellagra diagnosis.

She kept publishing.

---

### 3. The 1935 *Lancet* Paper — What She Wrote

The title was careful: *"Kwashiorkor: A Nutritional Disease of Children Associated with a Maize Diet."*

She did not claim to have solved the disease. She described what she had observed:

- The age of onset (typically 1–4 years, coinciding with weaning from breast milk)
- The geographical distribution (families subsisting on maize-based diets)
- The seasonal pattern (worse in the dry season when food variety was lowest)
- The clinical presentation (the specific pattern of oedema, skin changes, hair changes)
- The response to dietary intervention (improvements when protein-rich foods were added)

She provided the local name. She argued that naming a disease correctly — with a name that reflected its actual presentation and cause, rather than an incorrect analogy to a different disease — was an ethical obligation of medicine.

*"It seems possible that this condition is not pellagra but is a specific deficiency disease of which the predominant pathological feature is oedema."*

That sentence launched a field.

The full biochemical understanding of protein-energy malnutrition — the albumin hypothesis, the free radical theory of kwashiorkor, the role of aflatoxins, the debate between marasmus and kwashiorkor as presentations of the same underlying spectrum — developed over the following 60 years on the foundation of that 1935 paper.

---

### 4. The War

In 1941, Dr. Williams was stationed in Singapore as a senior medical officer. On February 15, 1942, the British forces in Singapore surrendered to the Japanese army — the largest surrender in British military history.

Cicely Williams was taken prisoner.

She spent three and a half years in Changi Prison and Sime Road Camp, Singapore. The conditions were brutal — malnutrition (she knew what that looked like), disease, forced labour, and violence. She was interrogated, tortured, and held in solitary confinement.

She survived.

When the war ended in 1945 and she was liberated, she returned to work.

She did not discuss the prison experience in professional contexts. She had work to do.

---

### 5. After the War — The Second Career

From 1948 to 1951, Cicely Williams worked at the World Health Organization, where she helped establish the foundational frameworks for international maternal and child health policy.

From 1953 to 1955, she served as the first head of the Maternal and Child Health section of the WHO.

She subsequently worked in West Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Caribbean, training local physicians and public health workers in nutritional disease identification and treatment.

She retired — nominally — in her 60s. She continued lecturing, publishing, and advising well into her 80s.

In 1989, she received an honorary degree from the University of the West Indies.

She died in Oxford in 1992, aged 98.

---

### 6. The Jamaican Context

Kwashiorkor did not end in West Africa when Williams published her paper. It followed the patterns of poverty, colonialism, and agricultural dependence that shaped the Caribbean alongside Africa.

Cases of kwashiorkor and protein-energy malnutrition were documented in Jamaica and across the Caribbean throughout the 20th century. The clinical descriptions matched Williams's 1935 paper exactly.

A Jamaican physician identified a disease that was killing children in West Africa and the Caribbean. The medical establishment initially dismissed her. She kept publishing. The disease is now one of the best-characterised nutritional deficiency syndromes in global medicine.

Her name is not widely known in Jamaica.

That is the purpose of this lesson.

### Discussion Questions

1. Dr. Williams distinguished kwashiorkor from pellagra based on clinical observation alone — she had no laboratory equipment, no blood tests, no biochemical assays. She used pattern recognition, local knowledge, and careful documentation. What does this tell us about the relationship between sophisticated technology and careful observation in medical diagnosis?

2. The word "kwashiorkor" comes from the Ga language of Ghana and means "the sickness the older child gets when the new baby is born." Williams chose to keep the local name rather than rename the disease in Latin or Greek as was conventional in Western medicine. What is the significance of this choice? What does it say about whose knowledge gets credited in science?

3. Williams was a prisoner of war for 3.5 years under conditions of severe malnutrition and violence. She was an expert in the clinical presentation of malnutrition — she would have recognised her own condition with clinical precision. What does it mean to have expert knowledge of your own suffering? How do you think this shaped her work after liberation?

4. Kwashiorkor is caused by protein deficiency, not caloric deficiency alone. A child can have enough calories from carbohydrates and still develop kwashiorkor if protein intake is insufficient. Design a public health intervention for a community subsisting primarily on a maize-based diet. What foods would you recommend, why, and how would you make them accessible to families with very limited resources?
