# WIS-02: The Doctor Who Flew
## Women in STEM — Lesson 2

**Core concept:** Space medicine, chemical engineering, human physiology in microgravity  
**Duration:** 90 minutes  
**Subject:** Dr. Mae Carol Jemison  
**Date of record:** September 12–20, 1992 · Space Shuttle Endeavour · Mission STS-47

---

### Opening Statement

> *"Don't let anyone rob you of your imagination, your creativity, or your curiosity. It's your place in the world; it's your life. Go on and do all you can with it, and make it the life you want to live."*
> — Dr. Mae Jemison

On September 12, 1992, Space Shuttle Endeavour cleared the launch tower at Kennedy Space Center and rose into a clear Florida sky.

On board was a physician, a chemical engineer, a dancer, a Peace Corps veteran, and a woman who had wanted to be an astronaut since she was a child watching the Apollo missions from her living room in Chicago.

Dr. Mae Carol Jemison became the first Black woman to travel to space.

She was 36 years old.  
She had a BS in Chemical Engineering from Stanford University.  
She had an MD from Weill Cornell Medical College.  
She had served as a Peace Corps medical officer in Sierra Leone and Liberia, treating patients in remote clinics with limited equipment.  
She spoke English, Russian, Swahili, and Japanese.  
She was also a licensed dancer.

And when a reporter later asked her how it felt to finally be the "first," she answered with characteristic precision:

*"I was the first. I will not be the last."*

---

### 1. The Path No One Had Walked Before

Mae Jemison was born on October 17, 1956, in Decatur, Alabama. Her family moved to Chicago when she was three. From early childhood, she was fascinated by science — astronomy, anthropology, archaeology — and by dance.

She enrolled at Stanford at age 16. She graduated in 1977 with degrees in both chemical engineering and African and African-American studies. She then attended Cornell Medical School, graduating in 1981.

After her medical residency in Los Angeles, she joined the Peace Corps as a medical officer in West Africa, 1983–1985. She managed health care for Peace Corps volunteers and State Department personnel, and supervised a research project on the hepatitis B vaccine.

She applied to NASA's astronaut programme in 1985. Her first application was rejected. She applied again in 1987. She was selected out of approximately 2,000 applicants.

She became the first Black woman to be accepted into the NASA astronaut programme.

---

### 2. What Chemical Engineers Do in Space

Jemison's STS-47 mission was a joint US-Japan science mission focused on life sciences and materials processing in microgravity.

Her scientific work aboard Endeavour included:

**Bone cell research:** In microgravity, the human body begins to lose bone density at approximately 1–2% per month — faster than the most severe cases of osteoporosis on Earth. With no gravitational load, osteoblasts (bone-building cells) reduce activity while osteoclasts (bone-resorbing cells) continue. Jemison studied this process to understand the mechanisms and develop countermeasures.

**Frog embryo development:** She conducted the first amphibian development experiment in space — studying whether frog eggs fertilised in microgravity would develop normally. They did. The tadpoles developed with correct orientation and anatomy. This provided critical data on whether animals can develop in zero-g environments.

**Human vestibular system:** The inner ear's vestibular system uses gravity to determine up and down. In microgravity, this system is disrupted, causing space adaptation syndrome (effectively space sickness) in approximately 50% of astronauts. Jemison studied adaptation mechanisms.

The chemical engineering at the core of this work is the same at every scale — understanding how molecular and cellular systems respond to changes in environmental forces.

### 3. The Physics of Microgravity

Inside the orbiting Space Shuttle, astronauts experience **weightlessness** — not the absence of gravity, but the condition of being in continuous free fall.

The Shuttle orbited at approximately 296 km altitude. At that height, Earth's gravitational acceleration:

$$g = \frac{GM}{(R_E + h)^2}$$

$$g = \frac{(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})}{(6.371 \times 10^6 + 2.96 \times 10^5)^2} \approx 9.1 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Gravity at 296 km is approximately 93% of surface gravity. The crew was not weightless because gravity was absent — it was 93% of normal. They were weightless because the Shuttle, and everything in it, was falling toward Earth at exactly the same rate as the acceleration of gravity. The fall and the orbital velocity balanced. Everything experienced the same free fall simultaneously.

**Orbital velocity at 296 km:**
$$v_{orb} = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{R_E + h}} = \sqrt{\frac{(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})}{6.667 \times 10^6}} \approx 7,730 \text{ m/s}$$

27,828 km/h. The Shuttle completed one full orbit of Earth every 91 minutes.

In eight days in space, Dr. Jemison orbited Earth 127 times.

### 4. The Peace Corps Doctor

Before she flew to space, Dr. Jemison worked in West Africa with the United States Peace Corps medical programme.

In Sierra Leone and Liberia, she managed health services for volunteers working in isolated communities — treating malaria, cholera, schistosomiasis, and traumatic injuries, often without the diagnostic equipment she had trained with in American hospitals.

She also supervised a study of the hepatitis B vaccine in these populations — one of the first structured clinical trials of its kind in the region.

This is the part of the story that receives the least attention. The astronaut mission was eight days. The Peace Corps work was two years.

The medical skills developed in a remote clinic in West Africa — improvising treatment plans with limited resources, remaining calm in diagnostic uncertainty, building trust with communities that had no reason to trust the system sending you — are not different in kind from the skills required to manage a medical emergency on an orbiting spacecraft. The environments are different. The cognitive demands are not.

### 5. After the Mission

Jemison left NASA in 1993, one year after her spaceflight. She founded the Jemison Group, a technology consulting firm, and the Dorothy Jemison Foundation for Excellence (named for her mother), which runs the science camp programme "The Earth We Share" for students aged 12–16.

She holds nine honorary doctorates. She has been inducted into the National Women's Hall of Fame, the National Medical Association Hall of Fame, and the International Space Hall of Fame.

In 1993, she appeared on *Star Trek: The Next Generation* as a transporter operator — the first real astronaut to appear on Star Trek. When she was a child, Lieutenant Uhura, played by Nichelle Nichols, was one of the reasons she wanted to go to space. She told Nichols this. Nichols wept.

**Dr. Mae Jemison on why she brings dance into science:**

*"When I'm in the lab and I'm working, there's a certain rhythm to it — the same rhythm that's in a good piece of choreography. The best scientists I know move through their work the way a good dancer moves. They know when to be precise and when to let intuition lead."*

---

### 6. What She Proved

Not that Black women can become astronauts. That was never in question to anyone who had actually looked at the evidence — it required only that they be given the opportunity.

What she proved — documented, in the historical record — is that:

1. A Black woman who wanted to be a scientist from childhood could follow that path to its furthest extreme, if the institutions responsible for her education chose to support rather than obstruct her.

2. The combination of disciplines — medicine, engineering, dance, languages, international development — is not a distraction from scientific excellence. It is the profile of an exceptional scientist.

3. The word "first" is a measurement of a barrier, not a ceiling.

### Discussion Questions

1. Dr. Jemison studied both chemical engineering and African and African-American studies at Stanford. Several of her professors suggested she choose one or the other. She refused. How might her work in both fields have made her a better scientist and a better physician? Can you think of other examples where cross-disciplinary training produced insights that a single-discipline approach would have missed?

2. Bone density loss in microgravity occurs at ~1–2% per month. The International Space Station has had continuous human habitation since 2000. Long-duration missions to Mars would take approximately 9 months each way. Calculate the cumulative bone density loss for a Mars mission crew. What countermeasures exist, and how effective are they?

3. Dr. Jemison worked as a Peace Corps physician in Sierra Leone before becoming an astronaut. How do you think her clinical experience in low-resource settings shaped her approach to medical research in space — another environment where resources are limited and improvisation is essential?

4. Lieutenant Uhura (played by Nichelle Nichols on Star Trek) inspired Jemison to pursue space. Jemison's spaceflight inspired a generation after her. Trace this chain of inspiration. Who will the next person in this chain be? What responsibility does a scientist have toward students who might be watching them the way Jemison watched Uhura?
