### Opening Statement

> *"What I do is definitely detective work. We look for evidence. We have to piece it all together like forensic scientists of the sky."*
> — Dr. Mercedes Richards

In a Kingston school yearbook from the early 1970s, a classmate wrote this inscription next to Mercedes Davis's portrait: *"Mad Mercy! Her ambition is to obtain a Ph.D. in Astronomy!"*

She kept that promise.

Mercedes Tharam Richards grew up in Kingston, Jamaica, attended St. Hugh's High School — an all-girls institution where female scientists and mathematicians taught her that women do this work — and completed a Bachelor of Science in Physics at the University of the West Indies before leaving the island to pursue a doctorate at the University of Toronto. She went on to become a full Professor of Astronomy and Astrophysics at Penn State University, **President of the International Astronomical Union's Commission on Close Binary Stars**, and the holder of the **Musgrave Medal in Gold** — Jamaica's highest academic honour.

She did not photograph stars. She diagnosed them.

Using a technique called **Doppler tomography** — which she pioneered for an entirely new class of stellar systems — she produced the first three-dimensional maps of gas flowing between two stars locked in mutual orbit. She called it "taking a CAT scan of the stars." The imagery was precise: like a medical radiologist reading invisible anatomy from the outside, Richards used the spectrum of light to reconstruct physical processes that no telescope could photograph directly.

Over a forty-year career, she published more than 100 peer-reviewed papers, mentored generations of students, and served as the senior governing scientist for her entire sub-field in the global astronomical community.

Her name is not widely known in Jamaica.

### 1. Who She Was

Mercedes Tharam Davis was born on 14 May 1955 in Kingston, Jamaica. Her father, Frank Davis, was a police detective. He took her on nature walks and taught her to observe, to deduce, to follow evidence where it led rather than where expectation pointed. Her mother, Phyllis Davis, was an accountant — precision in numbers, rigour in record-keeping. The combination of forensic observation and mathematical exactness became the signature of her scientific method.

She attended St. Hugh's High School in Kingston, one of Jamaica's oldest all-girls secondary institutions. She later credited that environment directly:

*"Having those female teachers gave me a boost. As a young woman I could say, 'Hey, I can be like them.'"*

She completed her BSc in Physics at the **University of the West Indies, Mona**, graduating 1977. She then moved to York University in Toronto for her MSc in Space Science (1979), and to the University of Toronto for her PhD in Astronomy and Astrophysics, awarded 1986.

Her career: postdoctoral work at UNC Chapel Hill, then Assistant → Associate → Full Professor at the University of Virginia, then Professor at Penn State from 2002 until her death in 2016.

She married Donald St. P. Richards — a mathematician and statistician, also a professor at Penn State — in 1980. They had two daughters, Chandra and Suzanne.

She was fluent in French. She played violin. She read detective novels.

### 2. Binary Stars — What She Studied

Most stars in the universe are not alone. More than half of all stars exist in **binary systems** — two stars bound by mutual gravity, orbiting a shared centre of mass, bound together for millions or billions of years.

In a **close binary**, the two stars orbit so near to each other that they affect each other's physical structure. As a star ages, it expands. If it expands far enough, its outer layers begin to cross a critical gravitational boundary — the **Roche lobe** — beyond which its companion's gravity takes over.

**The Roche lobe** is the teardrop-shaped region of space within which material is gravitationally bound to each star. The radius of the Roche lobe relative to the orbital separation *a* is given approximately by the Eggleton formula:

$$\frac{r_1}{a} \approx \frac{0.49\, q^{2/3}}{0.6\, q^{2/3} + \ln(1 + q^{1/3})}$$

where $q = M_1/M_2$ is the mass ratio of the two stars.

At the inner tip of each lobe — the **Lagrangian point L1** — the gravitational potential of the two stars is exactly equal. A star that overfills its Roche lobe loses mass through L1. That mass streams toward the companion star at hundreds of kilometres per second.

**Algol binaries** (named for Beta Persei, the prototype) are systems where this stream impacts the companion star directly, without forming a full disk. They are some of the most common interacting binaries in the sky. The orbital period of Algol itself is 2.87 days — meaning the two stars complete a full orbit around each other roughly every three Earth days.

The orbital mechanics follows Kepler's Third Law:

$$P^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{G(M_1 + M_2)} a^3$$

where $P$ is the orbital period, $G$ is the gravitational constant, and $a$ is the orbital separation.

### 3. The Technique She Invented

Before Doppler tomography, the problem was this: the gas flowing between two stars is invisible in any direct image. A telescope looking at Algol sees a point of blended light. The gas stream, the impact region, the magnetic structures — none of it is resolvable spatially. The stars are far too distant.

But the gas is **moving**. And moving gas shifts the wavelength of the light it emits.

This is the **Doppler effect**: when a source of light moves toward you, its wavelength is compressed (blueshift); when it moves away, its wavelength is stretched (redshift). The amount of shift is proportional to the velocity:

$$\frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda_0} = \frac{v}{c}$$

As a binary system orbits, different components of the gas stream move toward and away from Earth at different velocities, and at different orbital phases they face Earth at different angles. By collecting spectra at many orbital phases — sometimes over 2,500 individual spectral exposures — and applying a reconstruction algorithm mathematically equivalent to the tomographic reconstruction used in medical CT scanning, Richards and her team could reconstruct a **velocity-space map** of the entire gas structure.

She stated it directly: *"Astro-tomography is very much like taking a CAT scan of the stars. We get many spectral observations from all around our patient — the binary system — as it moves in relation to the Earth. Then we add all those observations together. The resulting composite image shows us how the gas moves between the stars."*

Doppler tomography had been developed in the 1980s for **cataclysmic variable** binary systems containing white dwarf stars. **Mercedes Richards was the first astronomer to apply it to Algol-type binaries** — where mass transfer is direct-impact rather than disk-mediated. No one had done it before because, as she put it, *"it was not possible to get a direct image."* She made it possible.

She then extended the method from two dimensions to **three dimensions** using a technique she called the Radioastronomical Approach, adapting interferometric reconstruction algorithms from radio astronomy — the same mathematical tradition that images molecular clouds and pulsar emission regions. Her 3D tomograms showed, for the first time, that gas streams in Algol systems are not confined to the orbital plane. They have vertical structure. They are deflected upward and downward by the magnetic fields of the donor star.

---

### 4. What She Found

From her 2014 *Astrophysical Journal* paper — analysing over 1,300 time-resolved spectra from 13 Algol binary systems — the H-alpha tomograms identified eight distinct structural components in the gas between the stars:

1. Chromospheric emission from the cool mass-losing star
2. A gas stream following the gravitational trajectory from L1
3. A star-stream impact region
4. An emission or absorption bulge around the mass-gaining star
5. A Keplerian accretion disk (in some systems)
6. A hotter absorption zone
7. A disk-stream impact region
8. A hot spot

Her 3D work on Beta Persei and RS Vulpeculae revealed gas stream loops reaching ±155 km/s above and below the orbital plane, and coronal mass ejections at +150 km/s — evidence that the donor star's magnetic field was actively deflecting the gas flow even across hundreds of thousands of kilometres of space.

This was paradigm-shifting. Standard models treated gas transfer as a purely hydrodynamic flow. Richards demonstrated that magnetic forces were structural.

### 5. Honours and Legacy

| Woman Physicist of the Month | American Physical Society | July 2013 |
| Honorary Member, Sigma Pi Sigma | American Physical Society | 2012 |

The **Musgrave Medal in Gold** requires separate attention. The Institute of Jamaica was founded in 1879. Its Gold Medal has been awarded for over a century. By 2008, fewer than 15 scientists in Jamaica's entire modern history had received it. It is the highest academic honour the Jamaican state confers.

As **President of IAU Commission 42**, she was the senior governing scientist for the entire global field of close binary star research — the equivalent of sitting at the head of the table for every astronomer on Earth working in her specialty.

She organised the first IAU symposium dedicated exclusively to binary stars, held in Slovakia during her Fulbright sabbatical.

She donated to her former school, St. Hugh's High School in Kingston, specifically to fund laboratory upgrades and the science programme. She credited her former mathematics teacher Marjorie Thomas publicly: *"She played a very special role in my career. She gave me a strong foundation of mathematics that made it easier to understand complex topics in Physics and Astronomy."*

### 6. The Jamaican Foundation

Mercedes Richards began her scientific education at the University of the West Indies, Mona — the same institution Jamaican students attend today. Her BSc in Physics from UWI was the foundation for a PhD at the University of Toronto, a career at Penn State, and the presidency of a global scientific commission.

She was explicit about the structure of her confidence: an all-girls school with female science teachers. The environment in which she formed her scientific identity told her the work was possible for someone who looked like her. She went back and donated to that school.

She died on 3 February 2016 in Hershey, Pennsylvania, at the age of 60. Penn State published a formal memorial. The *Jamaica Gleaner* published an obituary in its Footprints series — which records Jamaicans whose lives were larger than their public recognition.

She was one of the most decorated Jamaican scientists of her generation. Her yearbook classmate was right.

### Discussion Questions

1. Doppler tomography reconstructs a 3D velocity map of gas flows using spectra collected at many orbital phases over time — it never directly images the gas at all. In what other fields of science do we reconstruct physical reality from indirect measurements rather than direct observation? What are the limitations and strengths of indirect reconstruction compared to direct imaging?

2. Richards credited an all-girls school with female science and mathematics teachers as a direct factor in her confidence to pursue physics. What does this tell us about how scientific identity forms? Is the availability of role models that look like you a scientific issue, an educational issue, or a social one?

3. Her doctoral supervisor at the University of Toronto was not a binary star specialist — Richards applied Doppler tomography to Algol systems because she identified the gap herself. What habits of mind allow a researcher to see that a technique developed in one context can be transferred to a different problem? How do you train that kind of lateral thinking?

4. Dr. Richards donated prize money and time back to St. Hugh's, crediting a specific teacher by name. Jocelyn Bell Burnell donated $3 million to fund underrepresented physics students. Patricia Bath returned to establish community ophthalmology programmes. What pattern do you observe? What does it suggest about the relationship between recognition and responsibility in science?
