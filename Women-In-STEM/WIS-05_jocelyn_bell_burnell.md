# WIS-05: The Signal Nobody Believed Was Hers
## Women in STEM — Lesson 5

**Core concept:** Radio astronomy, pulsar discovery, Nobel Prize attribution  
**Duration:** 90 minutes  
**Subject:** Dame Jocelyn Bell Burnell DBE FRS FRSE FRAS  
**Institution:** University of Cambridge, Mullard Radio Astronomy Observatory  
**Date of record:** August 6, 1967 · CP 1919 (now PSR J1921+2153)

---

### Opening Statement

> *"I thought it might be a very large satellite, because satellites were quite new then. But it was too deep in space. Then I thought it might be a white dwarf star oscillating. But it was too regular. So I called it LGM-1."*
> — Jocelyn Bell Burnell, 1967

> *"LGM. Little Green Men."*

On the night of August 6, 1967, Jocelyn Bell was a 24-year-old PhD student at Cambridge, analysing 30 metres of paper printout from the radio telescope she had spent two years helping to build. She noticed something in the data — a signal, sharp and repetitive, repeating every 1.33730 seconds.

She called it a "bit of scruff." She took it to her supervisor, Antony Hewish. He said it was probably interference from man-made radio sources.

She watched the signal for weeks. It was not interference. It was not a satellite. It was not anything in the catalogue of known astronomical objects. Its regularity exceeded any known natural phenomenon. For a brief, serious moment, the possibility was entertained that it was an artificial signal from an intelligent extraterrestrial source.

Bell found the source. She identified it as astrophysical. She characterised it. She discovered, with her supervisor, that it was the first **pulsar** — a rotating neutron star emitting regular pulses of radio waves. She then found three more pulsars before her supervisor announced the discovery.

In 1974, the Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded for the discovery of pulsars.

It was awarded to Antony Hewish. And to Martin Ryle, who led the radio astronomy group.

Jocelyn Bell received her PhD that same year. She was not included in the Nobel Prize.

---

### 1. Who She Is

Susan Jocelyn Bell was born on July 15, 1943, in Belfast, Northern Ireland. Her father was an architect who worked on the Armagh Planetarium and brought home books from the planetarium's library for his daughter.

She failed the examination that determined secondary school placement in Northern Ireland — the 11-plus — but her parents sent her to a Quaker boarding school in England where she thrived. She then attended Glasgow University, where she was one of the only women studying physics. She later recalled that when she walked into lectures, male students would stamp their feet and whistle — a form of harassment she navigated by walking in calmly and sitting down.

She received her bachelor's degree in 1965 and moved to Cambridge to pursue a PhD under Antony Hewish. Her assigned task: help design and build a radio telescope specifically intended to study a type of radio source called quasars.

The telescope she helped build covered 1.6 hectares — roughly the area of 57 tennis courts. She spent two years hammering stakes, stringing wire, and connecting electronics. When it was complete, she was assigned to analyse its output: 30 metres of paper chart data per day.

She analysed all of it. By hand.

---

### 2. The Discovery — Step by Step

**July 1967:** Bell notices a strange, faint signal on the chart paper. It recurs at the same position in the sky. She marks it.

**August 6, 1967:** She has a higher-speed chart running on that section of sky. The signal resolves: sharp pulses, precisely 1.33730 seconds apart.

**September–October 1967:** She monitors the signal for two months. It is stable. It follows the sidereal day (the rotation period of the stars relative to Earth, approximately 23 hours 56 minutes), confirming it is astronomical, not terrestrial interference.

**November 1967:** She and Hewish begin seriously investigating. The "LGM" designation is used internally. Bell remains skeptical of the extraterrestrial intelligence hypothesis and keeps looking for alternative explanations.

**December 1967:** Bell discovers a second pulsar — in a completely different part of the sky. This eliminates the LGM hypothesis. One source might be a beacon. Two independent sources with different periods cannot both be directed at Earth. They are natural phenomena.

**January–February 1968:** She discovers two more pulsars. The paper announcing the discovery is published in *Nature* on February 24, 1968. Six authors. Bell is second author.

**The physical explanation** — proposed by Thomas Gold and Franco Pacini, confirmed by subsequent observation — is that pulsars are rotating neutron stars emitting a narrow beam of radio waves from the magnetic pole. Each time the beam sweeps past Earth, we detect a pulse. Like a lighthouse.

---

### 3. The Nobel Prize Question

The 1974 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Antony Hewish "for his decisive role in the discovery of pulsars" and to Martin Ryle for aperture synthesis radio astronomy. Bell was not included.

The Nobel Committee's position: Hewish was the principal investigator of the project, and the Nobel is typically awarded to the project leader.

The scientific community was not unanimous on this. Fred Hoyle — himself a Nobel laureate — publicly stated that Bell deserved to share the prize. Others argued that denying the prize to the student who found the signal in the data and identified it as significant was indefensible regardless of the institutional structure.

Bell's own position, stated publicly and consistently over the decades:

*"I believe it would demean Nobel Prizes if they were awarded to research students, except in very exceptional circumstances, and I do not believe this is one of them."*

She has never expressed bitterness publicly. She has continued to do exceptional science for more than 50 years since the discovery. She has been awarded:

- The Royal Medal (2015)
- The Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics (2018) — $3 million. She donated the entire amount to the Institute of Physics to fund scholarships for underrepresented students in physics.
- Dame Commander of the Order of the British Empire (DBE)
- President of the Royal Society of Edinburgh
- President of the Institute of Physics
- President of the Royal Astronomical Society

The woman who was not awarded the Nobel Prize has spent five decades being awarded nearly everything else physics has to give — while simultaneously donating her prize money to make physics more accessible to people who look like she looked in 1967: young, overlooked, and paying attention to data no one else thought was important.

---

### 4. What Paying Attention Means

The discovery of pulsars was made not because of sophisticated equipment (the telescope was made of wire, stakes, and labour), not because of advanced analysis software (there was none), and not because of seniority or prestige.

It was made because Jocelyn Bell read every centimetre of chart paper herself and noticed something faint, repeating, anomalous.

She noticed it.

In science, this is called **observational acuity** — the ability to see what is there when looking, rather than seeing what you expect to be there. It is trained by attention, practice, and a refusal to dismiss the unexplained.

Bell had been told the signal was interference. She could have accepted that explanation. She had 30 metres of chart paper per day and thousands of signals to sort through. The scruff appeared in the space of a couple of centimetres on a chart.

She did not accept the easy explanation. She kept watching.

---

### 5. The Breakthrough Prize Donation

In 2018, Jocelyn Bell Burnell was awarded the Breakthrough Prize — one of the largest science prizes in existence, worth $3 million.

Her response: she donated the entire amount to the Institute of Physics to fund PhD studentships for students from underrepresented groups — women, refugees, and people from minority ethnic backgrounds.

Her stated reason: *"I don't need the money myself and it seemed to me that this was actually a way of making a lasting contribution to science — by increasing the diversity of the people doing science."*

The woman who was excluded from the Nobel Prize created a fund to ensure that fewer scientists would be excluded from the field.

This is the definition of responding to injustice with construction rather than destruction.

### Discussion Questions

1. Bell noticed the pulsar signal in approximately 2 centimetres of chart paper in the middle of thousands of metres of data. She was not assisted by a computer or automated flagging system. What habits of mind and practice enable this kind of observational acuity? How can a student develop them?

2. The Nobel Prize was not awarded to Bell. She said she accepted this without bitterness and donated her subsequent prize money to fund underrepresented students. Is her response admirable, or does it let an injustice stand unchallenged? Is it possible to do both — accept a decision while still naming it as wrong?

3. The LGM hypothesis — that the signal might be from extraterrestrial intelligence — was taken seriously briefly at Cambridge. Bell rejected it not emotionally but empirically: she found a second pulsar in a different sky location and concluded that two independent natural sources were more plausible than two alien civilisations both signalling Earth. Walk through this logic. Why does the discovery of a second pulsar rule out the alien hypothesis more effectively than any other evidence?

4. Bell donated $3 million to fund scholarships for underrepresented students in physics. In 50 years, some of those students will make discoveries. Some of those discoveries may win Nobel Prizes. If that happens, what is the correct way to credit the history that made those discoveries possible?
