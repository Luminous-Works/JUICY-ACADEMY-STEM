### Opening Statement

> *"The Moon has no wind, no rain, no plate tectonics. Everything that has ever hit it is still there. It is the Solar System's memory."*

The Moon is the most-observed object in the night sky and the least changed solid body accessible to observation in the Solar System. Its surface preserves an impact record spanning 4 billion years. Every crater is an event — an asteroid or comet impact — that on Earth would have been erased by weathering, plate tectonics, and time.

Looking at the Moon through a telescope is looking directly at Solar System history.

From Jamaica — latitude 17–18°N — the Moon arcs high and clear across the southern sky. The large-aperture telescopes at Mona and the community instruments at CIMH give Caribbean students unobstructed views of one of the best-studied geological surfaces in the Solar System.

This module is built around the **Lunar 100** — a list compiled by Charles Wood (Sky & Telescope) of the 100 most instructive and interesting features on the Moon, arranged from the obvious to the subtle, designed as a progressive observational challenge.

### 1. How the Moon Formed — The Giant Impact Hypothesis

**The hypothesis:**

Approximately 4.5 billion years ago — within the first 100 million years of the Solar System — a Mars-sized protoplanet called **Theia** collided with the proto-Earth at an oblique angle.

The impact:
- Vaporised much of the Earth's mantle
- Ejected a massive cloud of debris into Earth orbit
- This debris accreted rapidly into the Moon

**Evidence:**

The Giant Impact hypothesis explains three anomalies that had previously been puzzling:

**1. The Moon is nearly devoid of volatile elements**  
(hydrogen, carbon, nitrogen, water) — the high-energy impact boiled them away. Earth's crust retains more volatiles because the impact was partial. Subsequent analyses of Apollo samples and lunar meteorites confirm the Moon's extreme dryness.

**2. The Moon's composition is similar to Earth's mantle**  
(not Earth's core, which is iron-nickel). If the Moon formed from impact ejecta, it would be predominantly mantle material — which it is.

**3. The Earth-Moon system's angular momentum**  
is consistent with an oblique high-energy impact. A Moon accreted from the general solar nebula would not produce this specific angular momentum.

**Note on lunar water:**  
Subsequent spacecraft missions (especially LCROSS — the 2009 Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite) confirmed water ice in permanently shadowed polar craters. This water likely came from later cometary and asteroidal impacts after the Moon cooled. It does not contradict the volatile-poor formation story.

### 2. Lunar Structure

The Moon is geologically stratified:

| Layer | Thickness/Depth | Composition |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| Crust | 0–50 km | Anorthosite (highland regions); basalt (mare regions) |
| Upper Mantle | 50–400 km | Olivine, pyroxene (partially molten early in history) |
| Lower Mantle | 400–1,100 km | Silicate rock, partially solid |
| Outer Core | 1,100–1,300 km | Liquid iron-sulphide (small, electrically conductive) |
| Inner Core | 1,300–1,740 km | Solid iron (radius ~240 km) |

**Why the Moon's interior matters:**

The Moon today is essentially geologically dead. It has no plate tectonics, no active volcanism, no global magnetic field (though it had one early in its history). This death was caused by its small size: small bodies cool faster than large ones (surface area to volume ratio). The Moon's interior cooled and solidified relatively rapidly.

The lack of a global magnetic field means the Moon has no protection from solar wind — which is one reason its surface regolith (soil) is charged with solar wind implanted particles, including helium-3 (a potential fusion fuel).

### 3. Impact Cratering — Reading the Moon's History

**The cratering record:**

Every crater on the Moon was made by an impact. The crater dimensions depend on the impactor's size, speed, and angle.

**Crater morphology:**

Small craters (< 15 km): **simple craters** — bowl-shaped, constant depth:diameter ratio (~1:5)

Larger craters (15–300 km): **complex craters** — central peak (rebound of the crust), terraced walls, shallower profile. Tycho (85 km) and Copernicus (93 km) are classic examples.

**Impact basins** (> 300 km): The multi-ring basins — Mare Imbrium (1,145 km), Mare Orientale (930 km) — represent the largest impacts in the Moon's history. Their concentric ring structures are raised ridges from the impact shock propagating through the crust.

**Age from crater density:**

The older a surface, the more craters it has accumulated. This allows relative dating: a region with few craters is younger than a region covered in craters.

Absolute dates came from Apollo samples. The Imbrium Basin (visible as the dominant circular mare on the near side) formed approximately **3.85 billion years ago** — a date cross-checked between the radiometric age of Apollo 15 basalts and the crater count on Imbrium's floor.

**The Late Heavy Bombardment:**

The Moon's heavily cratered highlands preserve the signature of an early period (~4.1–3.8 Gya) when the impact rate was dramatically higher — possibly caused by Jupiter and Saturn's orbital migration destabilising the asteroid belt. This **Late Heavy Bombardment** (or Lunar Cataclysm) also hit Earth, but Earth's geological activity has erased all evidence.

### 4. Mare Basalts — The Dark Seas

The dark regions visible to the naked eye are called **maria** (Latin: seas — the early astronomers thought they were bodies of water). They are solidified basalt lava plains.

**Formation:**

After the heavy bombardment, the large impact basins remained as deep topographic depressions. The Moon's interior, still partially molten in the mantle, produced **volcanic eruptions** that flooded these basins with low-viscosity basaltic lava.

This volcanism occurred primarily between 3.9 and 3.0 billion years ago. The last significant volcanic activity is dated to approximately 1.2 billion years ago (some evidence suggests even more recent activity at ~100 million years). The Moon's interior is now too cold for significant volcanism.

**Mare chemistry:**

Mare basalts are rich in:
- Iron (FeO): ~15–25%
- Titanium (TiO₂): up to 10% in high-Ti basalts
- Silicon, magnesium, calcium

They are depleted in:
- Aluminium (concentrated in the highland anorthosite)
- Volatiles (water, carbon, nitrogen — consistent with the Giant Impact)

### 5. The Lunar 100 — An Observational Programme

The **Lunar 100** is a list of 100 lunar features compiled by Charles A. Wood and published in Sky & Telescope (April 2004), designed as a structured observational challenge analogous to the Messier List for deep-sky objects.

Features are numbered L1 through L100, increasing in difficulty — from the obvious to the subtle.

**The First Ten (Primary targets for any Caribbean telescope):**

| # | Feature | Type | Significance |
|---|---------|------|-------------|
| L1 | The Moon itself | Full disc | Human-eye resolution shows maria and highlands |
| L2 | Tycho | Impact crater (85 km) | Youngest large crater; bright rays visible at full moon |
| L3 | Clavius | Impact crater (225 km) | Three craters along curved arc on floor |
| L4 | Copernicus | Impact crater (93 km) | Central peak, terraced walls; textbook complex crater |
| L5 | Mare Imbrium | Impact basin (1,145 km) | Youngest major basin; smooth lava floor |
| L6 | Orientale Basin | Multi-ring basin | Best example of multi-ring impact structure; near limb |
| L7 | Schröter's Valley | Sinuous rille | Largest sinuous rille; evidence of lava channel |
| L8 | Aristarchus Plateau | Volcanic plateau | Most reflective area on Moon; possible outgassing |
| L9 | Marius Hills | Volcanic dome field | Best example of lunar volcanic dome group |
| L10 | Hyginus Rille | Rille with craters | Possible chain of collapsed lava tubes |

**Selected Caribbean Visibility Targets:**

*These features are well-positioned for observation from Jamaica (17–18°N latitude) at optimal lunar phases:*

**Tycho (L2):**  
A fresh, 108 million year old crater 85 km in diameter in the southern highlands. At full moon, its **ray system** (impact ejecta streaks) extends across 2,000 km of the lunar surface — a spectacular naked-eye feature. Under oblique illumination at first or last quarter, the crater walls and central peak resolve clearly.

**Mare Crisium:**  
An isolated circular mare basin near the eastern limb, 556 km in diameter. Useful for demonstrating **libration** — the Moon's slight apparent wobble due to its elliptical orbit allows Crisium to move toward and away from the limb over the monthly cycle. Observe its position relative to the limb at different lunations.

**Vallis Schröteri (Schröter's Valley):**  
The Moon's largest sinuous rille — a channel carved by flowing lava. Approximately 160 km long, 10 km wide at its widest. The large depression at its head (the Cobra's Head) is the source vent. This feature was identified by Johann Schröter in the 18th century.

**The Straight Wall (Rupes Recta):**  
A linear fault scarp 110 km long, near Mare Nubium. Under the correct lighting (1–2 days after first quarter; 1–2 days before last quarter), it casts a distinct shadow, appearing as a perfectly straight dark line. Under the reverse illumination it glows white against the mare floor.

### 6. Regolith — The Moon's Dusty Skin

The lunar surface is covered by **regolith** — a layer of broken and pulverised rock produced by billions of years of meteorite impacts. This "soil" has no biological component (soil on Earth is defined partly by its organic content).

**Properties:**

- Thickness: 2–8 m on the mare surfaces; 10–20 m in the ancient highlands
- Grain size: fine silt to gravel (median ~70 µm)
- Composition: glass beads (from impact melt), mineral fragments, agglutinates (welded clumps)
- Electrostatic charge: the absence of atmosphere means solar UV charges the surface; regolith grains levitate slightly above the surface at the terminator

**The problem for future exploration:**

Lunar regolith is sharp-edged (not water-rounded like Earth sand). The glass shards cut through anything they penetrate. Apollo astronauts' suits degraded rapidly. The regolith coated optical instruments, jammed mechanisms, and proved hazardous to breathe.

Managing lunar regolith is one of the primary engineering challenges for sustained human presence on the Moon.

### 7. The Moon and Caribbean Astronomy

**Observational access from Jamaica:**

The Moon reaches declinations between approximately -28° and +28° through the month. From Jamaica's latitude (~17.5°N), the Moon is visible high in the sky for most of its orbit — excellent observational geometry.

**Key lunar events observable from Jamaica:**

- **Total lunar eclipses**: occur when Earth's shadow falls on the Moon. Visible from anywhere the Moon is above the horizon. The Moon turns red-orange during totality (sunlight refracted through Earth's atmosphere, the same effect that makes sunrises red on Earth).

- **Grazing occultations**: the Moon occasionally passes directly in front of stars near its limb. From specific latitude bands, the star appears to disappear behind lunar mountains and reappear in gaps — a technique used to map the Moon's limb profile with extraordinary precision.

- **Libration observation**: the Moon's apparent wobble over the monthly cycle brings different parts of the limb region into view. Systematic observation of Crisium's position relative to the limb quantifies libration geometrically.

**ACEAP and southern Caribbean access:**

The ACEAP (AUI Ambassador Program) Chile expeditions (2026 cohort — see project notes) give Caribbean students access to **Cerro Tololo**, home of CTIO, with instruments capable of lunar surface photography that rivals the professional range. The LROC (Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter Camera) QuickMap (publicly available) provides ~0.5 m resolution baseline comparison for ground-based images.

### 8. The Moon as Climate Archive

**Lunar polar ice (LCROSS, 2009):**

NASA's LCROSS mission deliberately impacted into Cabeus crater — a permanently shadowed crater near the lunar south pole. The impact plume was analysed spectroscopically. Result: confirmed presence of **water ice**, ethanol, sulphur dioxide, and other volatiles.

The water ice is preserved in permanently shadowed craters where temperatures remain below -170°C. It was deposited by comets over billions of years.

**Apollo Sample Archive:**

The six Apollo missions (1969–1972) returned 382 kg of lunar samples. These samples have been analysed with increasingly precise instruments for 50 years. They provide:
- Absolute radiometric ages for lunar surface formation events
- Isotopic composition of the early Solar System
- Evidence for the Late Heavy Bombardment
- Constraints on Earth-Moon formation models

No other planetary body has been sampled and returned to Earth.

### Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Mare (pl. maria) | Dark, relatively smooth lunar plain formed by basaltic lava flooding of impact basins |
| Regolith | Fragmented surface material on the Moon, produced by meteorite impact gardening |
| Simple crater | Bowl-shaped impact crater < ~15 km; depth:diameter ratio ~1:5 |
| Complex crater | Impact crater > ~15 km; central peak, terraced walls, shallow profile |
| Rille | Linear or sinuous surface channel on the Moon; either tectonic fault or collapsed lava tube |
| Speleothem | (Lunar analogy: lava tube) — the Moon's equivalent subsurface voids are lava tubes |
| Anorthosite | Calcium-rich feldspar rock forming the lunar highlands; ancient primordial crust |
| Late Heavy Bombardment | Period ~4.1–3.8 Gya of elevated impact rate; recorded in the heavily cratered highlands |
| Libration | The Moon's apparent wobble that brings ~59% of its surface into view over time |
| LROC | Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter Camera; NASA instrument producing 0.5 m resolution surface images |
