## 1. The Frame: What Is a Watershed?

A **watershed** is the land area in which rain falls and from which water drains into a common outlet — a stream, river, or coast. It includes mountains, slopes, forests, farms, and every human settlement on them.

Jamaica's watershed system has three layers of classification:

| Level | Count | What It Means |
|:---|:---:|:---|
| Hydrological Basins | 10 | Major drainage regions; each contains multiple WMUs |
| Watershed Management Units (WMUs) | 26 | Named for the principal river running through them; the operational management unit |
| Declared Watershed Areas (under WPA 1963) | 33 | Legally declared protection zones, from 1964–1983 |

The WRA also operates:
- **133 river gauging stations** (streamflow monitoring)
- **320 groundwater monitoring wells**
- Total active monitoring points: **453+**

> "Jamaica has healthy and optimally functional watersheds that are efficient and effective in the provision of ecosystem services for the benefit of all."
> — Vision, GOJ Watersheds Policy 2023

## 6. Groundwater Monitoring Network

The WRA monitors **320 groundwater well sites** across Jamaica. The limestone aquifer system (White Limestone Group) covers approximately **65% of Jamaica's land area** and is the primary drinking water source for most of the island.

### Key Aquifer Types

| Aquifer Type | Location | Characteristic |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Limestone Aquifer | Central/West Jamaica | High porosity karst; dominant source of potable water |
| Alluvial Aquifer | Coastal plains and interior valleys | Used for irrigation and domestic supply |
| Basal Aquiclude | Blue Mountains (Basins IX, X, II, I) | Volcanoclastic rock; limits groundwater storage |
| Coastal Aquifer | Littoral zones | Vulnerable to saltwater intrusion |

### Critical Karst Formations

| Formation | Role |
|:---|:---|
| **White Limestone Group** | Primary aquifer; high porosity; subject to intense dissolution |
| **Yellow Limestone Group** | Aquiclude; confining layer dictating subterranean conduit orientation |
| **Cockpit Country** | Global type-locality for polygonal karst; vital national aquifer |

## 7. Watershed Condition and Threats

### Status by Degradation Class (State of the Environment Report 2017)

| Degradation Class | % of Total Watershed Area | Characteristics |
|:---|:---:|:---|
| Least Degraded | 52% | Includes Rio Bueno-White River and Black River (two largest WMUs) |
| Less Degraded | 12% | Includes Great River and Cabarita River |
| Degraded | 22% | Predominantly eastern Jamaica |
| Severely Degraded | 14% | Rio Minho, Wagwater, Hope River, Yallahs |

### Primary Threats (Forestry Department / NEPA)

1. Encroachment by farmers and informal settlers into forested upper watersheds
2. Illegal logging — Jamaican Mahogany and Blue Mahoe
3. Infrastructure development (Highway 2000, toll roads)
4. Forest removal for bauxite and mineral extraction
5. Climate variability — longer dry periods, intensified hurricanes
6. Improper solid waste disposal and sewage
7. Agricultural chemical runoff (pesticides, fertilizers)
8. Unregulated quarrying and illegal mining

## 9. Lab Exercise: Watershed Forensic Audit

**Scenario:** A farming community in the upper Rio Minho watershed (Clarendon) reports declining water quality in their springs. Your task is to trace the contamination pathway.

**Step 1 — Spatial Context**
- Locate the Rio Minho WMU: Clarendon and St. Catherine, 799.32 km²
- Identify it as **Group 1 (Critical)** — highest priority for intervention
- Note: 30-year monthly mean = 4.09 m³/s; Q90 = 0.518 m³/s (low reliable yield)

**Step 2 — Aquifer Type**
- Determine if the suspect spring is in a limestone or non-limestone zone
- If limestone: contamination moves rapidly through karst conduits (no filtration)
- If non-limestone: slower surface runoff; erosion is the primary vector

**Step 3 — Instrument Deployment**
- Deploy **Mg·12** to test spring water: monitor TDS, pH, Hardness
- High Hardness (>300 ppm) = mature karst conduit system — contamination spreading fast
- pH drop below 6.5 = agricultural runoff or industrial discharge

**Step 4 — Cross-Reference**
- Compare readings against WRA Q90 baseline for Rio Minho @ Danks
- A flow deficit with rising contaminant levels = upstream abstraction or blockage

**Step 5 — Report**
- Identify contamination source by parish, land use, and upstream WMU zone
- Reference: GOJ Watersheds Protection Act (1963); NEPA enforcement mandate
