### Opening Statement

> *"Good artists copy; great artists steal."*
> — attributed to Pablo Picasso; quoted by Steve Jobs, 1996, describing Apple's relationship to Xerox PARC

> *"We were just smarter."*
> — Bill Gates, explaining Microsoft's success

Both of these men were, in fact, very smart. Both of them are also describing a version of events that leaves out the most important parts of the story.

This lesson is the parts they left out.

### 1. IBM — The Company That Ran the World, and the History It Prefers to Forget

**International Business Machines** was founded in 1911 and dominated American corporate computing from the 1950s through the 1980s. If the Manhattan Project produced the theoretical and physical foundations of the modern computer, IBM industrialised it.

By 1970, IBM had over 70% of the global mainframe computer market. The US Department of Justice filed an antitrust case against it in 1969. IBM's response was to drag the litigation for 13 years until the Reagan administration dropped the case in 1982. This is relevant: the pattern of a dominant technology company using its resources to outlast regulatory challenge rather than comply with it did not begin with Google or Facebook. It began with IBM.

But IBM has an older history that the corporate biography prefers to skip.

**Thomas Watson Sr.**, IBM's chairman, received the **Order of the German Eagle** — Nazi Germany's highest medal for foreign nationals — from Adolf Hitler in 1937. At the time, IBM's German subsidiary (**Dehomag**) was providing the **Hollerith punch-card tabulation machines** that the Nazi regime used to conduct the 1933 census, classify the German population by race, religion, and political affiliation, and organise the logistics of the Holocaust.

IBM historian Edwin Black documented this in *IBM and the Holocaust* (2001). The punch cards used at concentration camps had specific IBM codes: **Code 8** for Jews. **Code 11** for Gypsies. **Code 6** for the cause of death "special treatment" — a bureaucratic euphemism for execution.

IBM leased, not sold, its machines. IBM technicians serviced them. The leases were maintained throughout the war.

This is not an accusation. It is documented. The machines that organised the first industrial genocide were built by the company that would go on to dominate corporate computing for thirty years, train a generation of programmers, and eventually provide the infrastructure that Microsoft licensed its operating system through.

The chain of custody matters.

### 2. Xerox PARC — The Room That Invented Everything and Gave It Away

In 1970, Xerox Corporation — the photocopier company — opened the **Palo Alto Research Center** (PARC) in California. Their stated mission: invent the computer technology of the future.

They succeeded beyond any reasonable expectation.

Between 1970 and 1980, the researchers at Xerox PARC invented:

| Invention | Inventor(s) | Year |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| **Graphical User Interface (GUI)** — windows, icons, menus, pointer | Alan Kay, et al. | 1973 |
| **The mouse** (commercialised version) | Adapted from Doug Engelbart's original | 1973 |
| **Ethernet** — local area networking | Robert Metcalfe | 1973 |
| **Laser printer** | Gary Starkweather | 1971 |
| **Object-oriented programming** (Smalltalk) | Alan Kay | 1972 |
| **WYSIWYG word processing** | Charles Simonyi | 1974 |
| **Personal workstation computing** | Multiple | 1973 |

The Alto — PARC's prototype personal computer, built in 1973 — had a GUI, a mouse, a networked file system, and word processing software. It looked and functioned, in essential respects, like a modern computer.

Xerox did not successfully commercialise any of these inventions.

Their leadership did not understand what they had. The Xerox board was a copier company board. They saw PARC as a research vanity project. When PARC researchers demonstrated the Alto to Xerox executives, the executives did not grasp what they were seeing.

This is not primarily a story about corporate stupidity. It is a story about the gap between people who can imagine what something *is* and people who control whether it gets built — a gap that recurs throughout the history of technology and almost always disadvantages the inventor.

Alan Kay, who conceived much of the modern GUI and object-oriented programming, has spent decades diplomatically furious about what Xerox did with his work. His most quoted line: *"The best way to predict the future is to invent it."* His less quoted subtext: *"We invented it. Someone else predicted it."*

### 3. Apple — The Visit to PARC

In December 1979, a group from Apple Computer visited Xerox PARC.

The visit was not charity. Apple was in the process of raising venture capital. Xerox Development Corporation wanted to invest. The price of the investment was a demonstration of PARC's technology.

Steve Jobs was 24. He walked into the PARC demonstration room and watched researchers show him the Alto: the graphical interface, the mouse, the windows, the icons.

He later described it: *"I thought it was the best thing I'd ever seen in my life."*

Within months, Apple's engineers were reverse-engineering and improving on everything they had seen. The result was the **Apple Lisa** (1983), then the **Macintosh** (1984) — the first commercially successful personal computers with a graphical user interface.

Jobs was later confronted about appropriating PARC's work. His response — *"Good artists copy; great artists steal"* — is now one of the most quoted lines in Silicon Valley culture. It functions as a philosophy: the person who commercialises an idea owns it, regardless of who generated it.

Alan Kay was less philosophical. When Jobs told him the Mac was the first computer worth criticising, Kay replied: *"Yes, it's the first personal computer good enough to criticise. But you're five years late and $10 million short."*

Kay's point: PARC had already built it. Apple built it better and got it to market. Credit follows distribution, not origination.

This principle — **credit follows distribution** — is the organising logic of Silicon Valley's relationship to intellectual labour. It explains, in a single phrase, why the ENIAC programmers were support staff, why Xerox PARC researchers are historical footnotes, and why the names on the Forbes list are rarely the names of the people who did the foundational work.

### 4. Microsoft — The Operating System IBM Didn't Own

In 1980, IBM decided to build a personal computer. Their engineers were told to build it fast — under 18 months — using off-the-shelf components rather than proprietary IBM hardware. This was an unusual decision for a company that had built its monopoly on proprietary systems. IBM underestimated what they were giving away.

IBM needed an operating system. They approached **Gary Kildall** of Digital Research, who had written **CP/M** — the dominant PC operating system of the late 1970s. The meeting broke down. Accounts differ on why: Kildall was reportedly unavailable; a non-disclosure agreement issue with his wife; the details are disputed. The outcome is not.

IBM then approached **Bill Gates** at Microsoft. Microsoft was a small company that wrote programming languages. Gates did not have an operating system.

Gates told IBM he could provide one.

Then he called **Tim Paterson** at Seattle Computer Products, which had written a CP/M clone called **QDOS** (Quick and Dirty Operating System) to sell to computer manufacturers. Gates bought QDOS for **$50,000**. He did not tell Paterson it was for IBM.

He licensed it to IBM as **PC-DOS**. The critical term in the deal: Microsoft retained the right to license the operating system to other manufacturers.

IBM agreed. IBM believed the hardware was the valuable part.

When IBM-compatible clones began flooding the market — from Compaq, from dozens of Taiwanese manufacturers, from anyone who could buy Intel chips and a Microsoft license — every one of them ran **MS-DOS**. IBM had standardised the personal computer industry and handed the operating system rights to a 24-year-old in Seattle.

By 1990, Microsoft controlled the operating system running on more than 90% of the world's personal computers.

### 5. Mary Maxwell Gates — What the Official Biography Skips

The standard telling of Microsoft's origin is: brilliant Harvard dropout writes BASIC for the Altair 8800, spots IBM's need before anyone else, strikes the deal of the century.

This version is incomplete.

**Mary Maxwell Gates** — Bill Gates' mother — was serving on the **national board of United Way** in the early 1980s. So was **John Opel**, chairman of IBM.

They knew each other.

Whether Mary Gates directly facilitated the IBM connection has been debated. What is documented: she served on United Way's national executive committee alongside Opel from 1980 onward, IBM gave Microsoft the contract in late 1980, and Gates himself, in a 2019 interview, acknowledged that his family background and connections were significant advantages: *"I was lucky to be born to a mother who had contacts in the business community."*

Lucky. The word does careful work there.

Tim Paterson, whose QDOS became the foundation of the most valuable software company in history, received $50,000 and no royalties. He later sued Microsoft and settled for an undisclosed amount.

Gary Kildall, whose CP/M was the operating system the IBM PC could have run — which would have made Digital Research what Microsoft became — died in 1994, largely forgotten by the industry his work helped create. The circumstances of his death were disputed. He was 52.

The people who wrote the original code. The people who didn't have a mother on the right board. The people who signed the wrong contract or sat in the wrong meeting. Their names are footnotes. Their work is infrastructure.

### 6. Bill Gates — The Ethics File

Gates is widely regarded as one of the greatest philanthropists in human history. The **Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** has donated more than $50 billion to global health, education, and poverty reduction. It has funded vaccination campaigns that have saved millions of lives.

This is real. It is also not the complete picture.

**The antitrust case (1998–2001):**  
The United States Department of Justice, joined by 20 state attorneys general, sued Microsoft for monopolistic practices. The specific allegations: Microsoft had used its Windows monopoly to crush competitors by bundling Internet Explorer with Windows at no cost (destroying Netscape), pressuring computer manufacturers to exclude rival software, and attempting to "cut off Netscape's air supply" — a phrase from an internal Microsoft memo.

Gates' deposition was remarkable. Under oath, questioned about Microsoft's competitive practices, he was evasive, combative, and — on multiple occasions — appeared not to understand the meaning of words like "concerned" and "worried" when applied to his company's behaviour toward competitors.

Judge Thomas Penfield Jackson ruled that Microsoft had maintained its monopoly through anticompetitive means and ordered the company broken up. The breakup order was overturned on appeal. Microsoft was not broken up. It paid no significant financial penalty.

**Jeffrey Epstein:**  
Beginning in 2011 — three years after Epstein's 2008 conviction for soliciting a minor for prostitution — Gates met with Epstein on multiple occasions. The meetings were documented. Gates has given varying accounts of their purpose: that he was exploring Epstein's supposed philanthropy connections; that the meetings were a mistake; that his advisers recommended the connection.

Melinda Gates has stated that her husband's relationship with Epstein was one of the reasons she lost confidence in the marriage. The couple divorced in 2021 after 27 years.

**Vaccine IP and the pandemic:**  
During the COVID-19 pandemic, many countries in the Global South pushed for a waiver of intellectual property protections on vaccine technology, which would have allowed them to manufacture vaccines locally. The Gates Foundation initially opposed this waiver.

The position was eventually moderated. The episode raised a structural question: a private foundation with enormous influence over global health policy — run by one person, accountable to no electorate — was helping to determine whether poor countries could produce vaccines for their own populations.

This is not a question about Gates' intentions. It is a question about governance: who should make decisions about global public health, and what accountability structures should exist for those who do?

### 7. Silicon Valley — The Pattern

IBM, Xerox, Apple, Microsoft are not four separate stories. They are one story, told four times with the same structure:

1. **The foundational work** is done by people who are often not wealthy, often not well-connected, often not white men — the PARC researchers, the QDOS programmer, the punch-card engineers, the women who programmed the first computers.

2. **The commercialisation** is done by people who are almost always wealthy, almost always well-connected, almost always white men — who use legal, financial, and social capital to convert others' work into their own wealth.

3. **The credit** follows the commercialisation, not the origination.

4. **The wealth** concentrates among the commercialisers.

5. **The story** gets retold as individual genius: Gates in his garage, Jobs in his garage, Zuckerberg in his dorm room. The garages were real. The story is incomplete.

This is not an argument that Gates, Jobs, or any of these figures were not genuinely talented. They were. Talent is real. But talent is not sufficient to explain the outcomes. The connections, the capital, the legal structures, the racial and gender homogeneity of who gets funded and who gets listened to — these factors explain the distribution of outcomes just as much as individual ability.

In 2024, the top five technology companies by market capitalisation are all run by or founded by men. The majority are white. The majority attended elite universities. The vast majority of their early investors were white men from connected families.

The technology they built affects every human being on Earth. The room where it was decided looked like one kind of person.

That is the ethics problem. Not one bad actor. A system.

### Discussion Questions

1. IBM's Hollerith machines were used in Nazi Germany with IBM's knowledge and ongoing technical support. IBM has acknowledged the historical record but has not formally taken responsibility beyond acknowledging it happened. What is the appropriate corporate response to historical complicity in atrocity? Does it matter that the executives who made those decisions are long dead?

2. Xerox PARC invented the graphical user interface, the mouse, ethernet, the laser printer, and object-oriented programming — and failed to commercialise any of it. Apple commercialised the GUI and became the most valuable company in history. Who deserves the credit? Who deserves the money? Are these the same question?

3. The principle "credit follows distribution" — not origination — operates throughout the history of computing and throughout the music, film, and fashion industries. It systematically disadvantages inventors and benefits distributors. Is this a natural property of markets, or a specific legal and social construction that could be changed? What would change it?

4. The Gates Foundation has more influence over global public health policy than most national governments. It is accountable to no electorate. It was built on wealth accumulated through monopolistic practices that a federal court found illegal. How should we evaluate a philanthropy built on this foundation? Does the good it does change the ethical valence of how it was funded?

5. Silicon Valley is demographically homogeneous. The technology it produces affects everyone on Earth. Design a governance structure for large technology companies that ensures the people most affected by their products have meaningful input into how they are built. What obstacles would this face? What would it cost the companies, and what would it cost those currently excluded?
