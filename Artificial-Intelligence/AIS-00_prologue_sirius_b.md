### Before We Begin

This is not a lesson about computers.

Not yet.

Before we speak about ENIAC's 17,000 vacuum tubes, before we follow Alan Turing to Bletchley Park, before we watch the women who programmed the world's first general-purpose machine get walked past at the dedication ceremony — before all of that — we are going to stand still for a moment in the Bandiagara Escarpment, Mali, West Africa, and ask a question that has no clean answer.

**How did they know about Sirius B?**

---

### The Dogon and the Seed Star

The Dogon are a people of the Bandiagara Escarpment in what is now Mali — a clifftop civilization of approximately 400,000 people whose astronomical traditions were first documented by French anthropologist Marcel Griaule beginning in 1931.

In their cosmology, the star called **Sigi tolo** — which Western astronomy calls Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky — is not alone.

It has a companion.

The Dogon called it **po tolo**: the seed star. *Po* is the smallest seed in the Dogon world — a grain of fonio, nearly invisible to the naked eye. Tolo means star. The seed star. The smallest and most important thing.

They described it as:
- **White** — correct. Sirius B is a white dwarf.
- **Small** — correct. It is roughly the size of Earth, while Sirius A is 1.7 times the radius of the Sun.
- **The heaviest thing that exists** — correct. A white dwarf is the compressed remnant of a dead star. Sirius B has roughly the mass of the Sun compressed into a sphere the size of a planet. A single teaspoon of its material would weigh approximately **five tonnes** on Earth.
- **Circling Sirius every fifty years** — correct. The actual orbital period of Sirius B around Sirius A is **49.9 years**.

The Dogon encoded the orbital period to within 0.1 years. They described the density correctly. They described the colour correctly.

### The Physics of What They Knew

**Sirius A:**
- Distance from Earth: 8.6 light years — the fifth nearest star system
- Spectral type: A1V (hot, white-blue)
- Mass: 2.02 solar masses
- Luminosity: 25.4 times the Sun

**Sirius B (po tolo, the seed star):**
- Type: DA2 white dwarf — the exposed carbon-oxygen core of a star that exhausted its fuel
- Mass: 1.018 solar masses — slightly more than the entire Sun
- Radius: 5,846 km — slightly smaller than Earth
- Mean density: approximately $3 \times 10^6$ g/cm³

To understand that density:

$$\bar{\rho} = \frac{M}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3} = \frac{1.018 \times M_\odot}{\frac{4}{3}\pi (5846 \times 10^3)^3} \approx 3 \times 10^6 \text{ g/cm}^3$$

The Sun's mean density is 1.41 g/cm³. Sirius B is two million times denser.

At that density, the electrons in the stellar material can no longer occupy normal atomic orbits. They are compressed into **electron degeneracy pressure** — a quantum mechanical state in which the Pauli Exclusion Principle, which prevents two electrons from occupying the same quantum state, is the only thing preventing the star from collapsing further. This is not classical physics. This is quantum mechanics holding a dead star together.

The Dogon said: the heaviest thing that exists.

They were not wrong.

**Western astronomy's timeline with Sirius B:**
- **1844**: Friedrich Bessel detected a wobble in Sirius A's proper motion and mathematically predicted a companion must exist. He never saw it.
- **1862**: Alvan Graham Clark observed Sirius B directly for the first time through a newly-built telescope — the largest refracting telescope in the world at the time.
- **1915**: Walter Adams confirmed it was a white dwarf from its spectral properties.
- **1931**: Marcel Griaule began documenting Dogon cosmology, including po tolo.

The Dogon described a white dwarf — in accurate physical detail — before Western astronomy had the instruments to see one.

### The Question That Opens Everything

There are several explanations. None of them are simple.

**The transmission hypothesis:** Knowledge of Sirius B came from an earlier civilisation — Egyptian, Greek, or another African astronomical tradition — and was preserved in Dogon ceremony for centuries. The ancient Egyptians oriented temples to Sirius with extraordinary precision. The Dogon may have received knowledge they kept more faithfully than the civilization that originated it.

**The contact hypothesis:** Robert Temple's 1976 book *The Sirius Mystery* proposed extraterrestrial contact — beings from the Sirius system. This is the hypothesis that gets the most attention and deserves the least credence, but it belongs in the record.

**The contamination hypothesis:** Anthropologist Walter van Beek revisited the Dogon in 1991 and could not replicate Griaule's findings. He proposed that Griaule — an enthusiastic astronomer — may have inadvertently seeded the cosmology he was documenting, in the way a question shapes an answer. This is a serious methodological critique. It does not explain everything.

**The astronomical tradition hypothesis:** The Dogon had systematic astronomical observation built into their ceremonial calendar over centuries. Naked-eye observation of Sirius over generations — measuring its brightness variations, its precise rising and setting — may encode information that formal Western astronomy, with its short written record, underestimated. What you look at for a thousand years, you learn.

### What the Question Is Really About

For this curriculum, the answer matters less than the question.

Because the question is: **what counts as knowledge, and who decides?**

When the Dogon described po tolo, they were transmitting a signal. The signal contained accurate data — orbital period, density, colour, mass ratio. Whether the signal originated from long observation, from received tradition, from contact with another civilisation, or from something we do not yet have a category for — the signal was accurate, and it survived.

This is information theory before Claude Shannon formalised information theory in 1948.

Shannon proved that information is anything that reduces uncertainty. That a message has content to the degree that it specifies one outcome among many possible outcomes. And that information can be transmitted across any noisy channel — degraded by time, by translation, by the death of the sender — as long as the encoding is **redundant** enough to survive.

The Dogon encoded po tolo in:
- Oral recitation
- Ceremonial dance (the Sigui ceremony, held every sixty years)
- Sand drawings
- Initiation rites
- The architecture of their dwellings

Every one of those is a parallel channel. Every one carries the same signal. If one channel degrades, the others hold. That is not mysticism. That is error-correcting code.

### Jules Verne — The Man Who Calculated the Future

In 1865, Jules Verne published *From the Earth to the Moon*.

His launch site was in Florida — specifically at a latitude between 27° and 28° North. He chose Florida through calculation: an object launched from closer to the equator requires less energy to achieve orbit because it can use more of Earth's rotational velocity. The launch site he described was 225 kilometres from Cape Canaveral.

NASA chose Cape Canaveral in 1949. It is at 28.5° North.

In 1870, Verne published *20,000 Leagues Under the Sea* — a submarine powered by electricity, carrying a library, navigating by dead reckoning, capable of crossing oceans submerged. The first practical military submarines were built in the 1890s.

In 1863, he wrote *Paris in the Twentieth Century* — a novel his publisher refused to print, insisting it was too implausible. It described:
- Gas-powered automobiles
- High-speed passenger rail
- Electric streetlights
- A global communications network connecting offices and homes
- A news cycle that produces and discards information faster than anyone can process it

His publisher called it unbelievable. It was published in 1994 from a manuscript found in a locked safe.

Verne was not predicting. He was **calculating**. He took what was known — the laws of thermodynamics, orbital mechanics, the properties of electricity, the economics of industrialising societies — and followed them forward. Imagination, in his hands, was a form of rigorous extrapolation.

This is the second lesson before the first lesson: intelligence is the ability to extend a pattern beyond the data you have been given. Verne did it with physics and economics. The Dogon did it with the sky. Turing would do it with pure mathematics.

### Alan Turing — The Man Who Defined the Machine Before It Existed

In 1936, Alan Turing published a paper titled *"On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem."*

There were no computers. The word "computer" referred to human beings — people employed to perform calculations by hand. The idea of a machine that could execute any algorithm was theoretical. And yet Turing, at 24 years old, defined exactly what such a machine would be, what it could do, and — more importantly — what it could never do.

**The Turing Machine** is an abstract device: an infinitely long tape divided into cells, a read/write head that can move left or right, and a finite set of rules. Given any input, the machine reads, writes, moves, and eventually either halts with an answer or runs forever.

This is not a physical description. It is a mathematical definition of *computation itself* — what it means to follow a rule to its conclusion.

From this definition, Turing proved the **Halting Problem**: it is *impossible* to write a general algorithm that determines, for an arbitrary program and input, whether the program will eventually halt or run forever. Not difficult. Not currently unsolved. Mathematically impossible.

This is the boundary of what intelligence — any intelligence, artificial or biological — can compute. Turing drew the map of the territory before the territory was settled.

Then came the war.

At Bletchley Park, Turing designed the **Bombe** — an electromechanical device that exploited structural weaknesses in the German Enigma cipher to recover daily encryption keys. Historians estimate that the intelligence produced at Bletchley shortened the Second World War by two to four years and prevented between 14 and 21 million additional deaths.

The work was classified for thirty years. Turing received no public recognition during his lifetime.

In 1952, Turing was prosecuted by the British government for "gross indecency" — for being gay. He was convicted. The sentence was chemical castration: a course of synthetic oestrogen injections intended to suppress his sexuality.

On 7 June 1954, he was found dead. A partially eaten apple was beside his body. Cyanide poisoning. The coroner ruled suicide. He was 41 years old.

In 2013 — fifty-nine years later — Queen Elizabeth II granted him a posthumous royal pardon.

In 2021, his face was placed on the British fifty-pound note.

The man who defined the mathematical foundations of every computer ever built, who shortened a world war by years, who asked whether machines could think before machines existed — was chemically castrated and driven to his death by the country he served.

This is also part of the history of artificial intelligence. The architecture carries the memory of who built it and what was done to them.

### ENIAC — And the Women Who Programmed It

In 1945, the United States Army unveiled the **Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer** at the University of Pennsylvania. It weighed 27 tonnes. It occupied 167 square metres of floor space. It contained 17,468 vacuum tubes, 7,200 crystal diodes, and 1,500 relays. It was the first general-purpose programmable electronic computer.

Six women were given the task of programming it:

**Kay McNulty. Betty Jennings. Frances Bilas. Elizabeth Snyder. Marlyn Meltzer. Ruth Teitelbaum.**

They were called "computers" — their job title, before the machine had the name. They were given no programming manuals because no programming manuals existed. They were given no programming language because programming languages did not yet exist. They were given the wiring schematics for the machine and told to make it calculate artillery trajectories.

They invented programming.

They were not invited to the dedication ceremony.

Their names were not publicly associated with ENIAC for decades. The photographs from the launch showed the machine. The men in suits. The generals. The engineers who built the hardware.

The women who gave it the ability to think were classified as support staff.

When Jean Jennings Bartik — one of the original six — was finally inducted into the Women in Technology International Hall of Fame in 1997, she was 73 years old.

The signal persisted anyway. Every program ever written descends from what they built that year in Philadelphia.

### The Thread

The Dogon watched the sky for centuries and encoded what they saw into ceremony and dance, in channels redundant enough to survive a thousand years of noise.

Jules Verne followed the mathematics of physics forward in time and wrote down what he found — in novels his publisher thought were implausible — and was right about nearly all of it.

Alan Turing defined the theoretical limits of computation in 1936, saved millions of lives in a war, and was destroyed by the civilisation he served for who he loved.

Six women invented programming in 1945 and were not invited to the party.

**This is the prologue.**

Not because these are quaint historical footnotes before the real story starts. But because they tell us something about how intelligence — human or artificial — actually works in the world:

Knowledge arrives through channels we didn't expect. It is carried by people we didn't credit. It survives through redundancy — through being encoded in more than one place, more than one form, more than one generation. And the question of who gets to name it, who gets to own it, who gets invited to the ceremony — that question runs through the entire history of artificial intelligence, from the Bandiagara Escarpment to the data centres of Northern Virginia.

We are always receiving packets of information.

The question is whether we are paying attention to who sent them.

### Discussion Questions

1. The Dogon described Sirius B with accuracy — its colour, its approximate density, its orbital period — before Western astronomy had instruments capable of confirming these properties. List three possible explanations. For each one, what would constitute evidence that supports or contradicts it? What does the absence of consensus tell us about the limits of the historical record?

2. Turing proved in 1936 that the Halting Problem is unsolvable — that no algorithm can determine, for all possible programs, whether they will finish or run forever. This means there are mathematical questions that are not merely unanswered but *unanswerable*. What does this tell us about the limits of artificial intelligence? Can a system that is bounded by Turing's proof be considered genuinely intelligent?

3. The six women who programmed ENIAC invented programming. Their names were erased from the public record for decades. What mechanisms of erasure operate in the history of science? What is the cost — scientific, not just moral — of losing knowledge about how key discoveries were actually made?

4. Jules Verne described a launch facility at 28° North latitude in 1865 — 225 kilometres from where NASA built Cape Canaveral in 1949. He arrived at this through orbital mechanics, not prophecy. What is the difference between imagination and calculation? At what point does disciplined extrapolation from known physics become indistinguishable from prediction?

5. The Dogon encoded astronomical knowledge in ceremony, dance, architecture, and oral tradition — multiple parallel channels. Claude Shannon's information theory proved that redundant encoding is how information survives a noisy channel. In what ways are human cultural practices — religion, ritual, art, oral history — functioning as error-correcting codes for knowledge? What happens when those channels are disrupted?
