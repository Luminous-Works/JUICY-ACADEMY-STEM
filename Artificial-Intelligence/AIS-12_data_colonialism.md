### Opening Statement

> *"What does it mean that the same structural logics of extraction that drove colonialism now drive the economy of data?"*
> — Nick Couldry and Ulises Mejias, *The Costs of Connection*, 2019

There is a question you have never been asked by any company whose app is on your phone.

What is this worth?

Not to you — what you get from it. To them — what they get from you. The photographs you upload, the searches you run, the videos you watch for seven seconds before scrolling past, the exact point on a map where you stopped moving for forty-five minutes every Sunday morning, the friends you text at 2 AM when you cannot sleep, the products you look at and do not buy, the products you look at and buy anyway — all of it is recorded. All of it is analyzed. All of it generates value. That value does not flow to you.

This arrangement is not an accident. It was designed. The people who designed it understood what they were doing. They also understood that if you fully understood what they were doing, you might not agree to it — so the agreement is buried in Terms of Service documents that average 36 pages and are written at a 14th grade reading level, and you are given exactly two options: agree, or do not use the service.

This is not a consumer experience problem. This is a structural problem. And it has an exact historical parallel.

### 1. The Framework: Data Colonialism

In 2019, media scholars **Nick Couldry** (London School of Economics) and **Ulises Mejias** (State University of New York, Oswego) published *The Costs of Connection: How Data Is Colonizing Human Life and Appropriating It for Capitalism*. Their central argument is this:

**Colonial powers extracted physical resources from the Global South.** Land was seized. Labor was enslaved or coerced. Sugar, cotton, tobacco, gold, uranium, copper, rubber, timber, and minerals were extracted from Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Caribbean. The resources flowed from periphery to center. The value — the manufactured goods, the capital accumulation, the industrial development — accumulated at the center. The periphery was left with depleted land, disrupted societies, and economic structures organized around export to benefit someone else.

**Digital capitalism extracts behavioral data with the same structural logic.** The resource is different — it is not land or labor in the 18th-century form — but the directional flow is identical. Behavioral data is generated by billions of people in the Global South: in India, Nigeria, Brazil, Indonesia, Jamaica, Trinidad, Kenya, Ghana, the Philippines. It flows to servers owned by five companies headquartered in California and Washington State. The value — the advertising revenue, the AI training data, the political influence, the financial returns — accumulates at the center. The periphery continues generating the resource.

Couldry and Mejias are not arguing that digital platforms are exactly the same as the transatlantic slave trade. They are arguing that the structural logic — the asymmetric extraction of value, the absence of consent or compensation, the conversion of human life into a resource for someone else's accumulation — is a colonial logic, and that recognizing it as such is necessary for understanding what kind of intervention might change it.

This framework is the lens for everything that follows in this lesson. Keep it in mind.

### 2. The Business Model — Made Explicit

Facebook is free to use. Instagram is free. WhatsApp is free. Google Search is free. YouTube is free. TikTok is free. Twitter/X is free. Gmail is free.

These are not acts of generosity. They are not subsidized public goods. They are businesses generating hundreds of billions of dollars in annual revenue. Understanding where that revenue comes from requires understanding what the product is.

**The product is not the software.** The product is you — specifically, a detailed behavioral profile of you, updated continuously in real time, that allows advertisers to show you an advertisement at the precise moment and in the precise context in which you are most likely to respond to it.

Here is what that profile contains for an average user of Meta's platforms:

- **Age, gender, location, language, education level, relationship status, family composition** — from your profile
- **Political orientation** — inferred from pages you follow, content you engage with, news you share
- **Religious beliefs** — inferred from group memberships, holiday-related content, language patterns
- **Economic status and purchasing power** — inferred from location, employment, spending patterns if linked accounts exist, device model (iPhone users are worth more to advertisers than Android users in most markets)
- **Psychological profile** — derived from the Cambridge Analytica research basis: what content triggers your engagement, whether you respond more to fear, outrage, validation, or aspiration
- **Social graph** — who you know, how frequently you interact with them, the strength of your relationships
- **Emotional state** — Facebook's internal 2014 study (published without user consent) demonstrated they could systematically affect users' emotional states by adjusting the content shown in their feeds. If they can affect it, they can observe it.
- **Health indicators** — searches related to medical conditions, purchases of health products, mentions of symptoms in messages (Facebook claimed it does not read private messages; this claim has been challenged by researchers and by Facebook's own filed patents)
- **Location history** — not just your home city, but everywhere your phone has been, including medical facilities, religious institutions, political meetings, and the homes of specific individuals

Meta's revenue in 2023 was **$134.9 billion**. Approximately **97%** of that revenue came from advertising. Meta has approximately 3.2 billion daily active users. The arithmetic: each user generates roughly **$42 per year** in advertising revenue for Meta.

This is not counting the value of the data for training AI models, for political influence operations (whether sold or operated by Meta internally), or for the secondary market value of the data if it is shared with or sold to third parties — which Meta's terms of service permit under certain conditions.

You generate this value. You receive none of it. The Terms of Service you agreed to — which most users have never read — transferred your implicit property rights in your behavioral data to Meta at the moment of signup.

### 3. Surveillance Capitalism — The Theoretical Framework

In 2019, Harvard Business School professor and social psychologist **Shoshana Zuboff** published *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism: The Fight for a Human Future at the New Frontier of Power*. It is 700 pages long. It is the most thorough analysis of the economic logic of data extraction published to date.

Zuboff's framework has three core concepts:

**Behavioral data as raw material.** Just as cotton and sugar were raw materials extracted from the Caribbean and processed into commodities elsewhere, human behavioral data is a raw material extracted from human activity — browsing, searching, clicking, moving, communicating — and processed into prediction products.

**Prediction products.** These are what surveillance capitalism actually sells. An advertiser does not buy "Caribbean users who clicked on a shoe ad." An advertiser buys a prediction: *this specific person, in this specific context, at this specific moment, has an X% probability of purchasing this product if shown this advertisement in this format.* The prediction is based on the behavioral data. The more detailed the data, the more accurate the prediction. The more accurate the prediction, the more the advertiser will pay. The economic incentive for the platform is to collect as much behavioral data as possible — because behavioral data is what makes predictions valuable.

**Behavioral surplus.** Zuboff's most important concept. Platforms collect more behavioral data than is necessary to improve the service they offer. If Google's goal were only to improve search results, it would need data about your search queries. It does not need data about your location while you search, your subsequent browsing behavior, your physical movement, and your email content. The excess — the behavioral data collected beyond what is necessary to provide the service — is "behavioral surplus." It belongs to Google, not to you. You generated it. You did not consent to its specific uses. You did not receive compensation for it. It is the extraction.

This economic logic produces a specific incentive structure that explains several phenomena that might otherwise seem like corporate bugs:

- **Why social media platforms optimize for outrage and engagement over accuracy:** content that triggers strong emotional responses generates more behavioral data — more clicks, more shares, more time-on-platform, more observable reactions — than calm, accurate content. The platform's economic interest is in engagement, not in truth.
- **Why platforms fight regulation:** regulation that requires meaningful consent, data minimization, or user compensation would reduce behavioral surplus. Every piece of information you do not generate, or that you generate but the platform cannot keep, reduces the value of the prediction product.
- **Why "just delete your account" is inadequate as a solution:** Zuboff's analysis shows that the problem is not your individual use of any specific platform. The problem is the structural economic logic that makes behavioral extraction the dominant business model. You can delete your Instagram. You cannot opt out of the economic system that has organized itself around your behavioral data.

### 4. The Language Problem — Who Gets Left Out

Here is a fact about the AI systems that are increasingly governing access to education, employment, and public services worldwide:

**The training data is overwhelmingly in English.**

W3Techs, which monitors website content language, reports that as of 2024, **English represents approximately 56% of all indexed web content.** The second-ranked language is Russian at approximately 5%. Spanish is third at approximately 4%. German is fourth at approximately 4%.

Swahili — spoken by **200 million people** across East and Central Africa — represents approximately **0.01%** of web content. Haitian Creole — spoken by approximately 12 million people — is not separately tracked but represents a fraction of a fraction. Jamaican Patois (Jamaican Creole) — spoken by the majority of Jamaica's 3 million people as their first language — is similarly negligible in internet data. Most Caribbean creole languages, indigenous Caribbean languages, and African languages face the same absence.

This matters because **large language models and AI systems are trained on this data distribution**. A model trained on 56% English content and 0.01% Swahili content has learned a profound amount about the world as described in English and very little about the world as described in Swahili, Haitian Creole, or Jamaican Patois.

The practical consequences:

**Performance disparity.** AI systems — including translation tools, speech recognition, text generation, sentiment analysis, and automated content moderation — perform dramatically worse on underrepresented languages. A medical AI system tested primarily in English will perform worse when a Jamaican patient describes symptoms in Patois. A speech recognition system trained primarily on North American English accents will fail more often on Caribbean English. These are not cosmetic failures. They are material failures with consequences for health, education, employment, and safety.

**Cultural bias.** A model trained predominantly on English-language text has internalized an English-language worldview — its assumptions about social structures, family formations, religious practices, economic systems, and political norms. When that model is deployed to generate content, moderate speech, or make recommendations in a Jamaican, Haitian, or Kenyan context, it applies English-language norms to non-English-language contexts.

**Economic exclusion.** AI tools that do not work well in your language do not reduce your workload. Voice assistants you cannot use, translation tools that fail you, autocomplete that does not understand you — these are not neutral failures. They represent a technological infrastructure built for English speakers and sold to everyone.

The World Wide Web Consortium estimated that if you are a native speaker of a language spoken by fewer than 10 million people, there is roughly an 80% chance that the AI tools shaping the digital services you encounter were not tested in your language and perform poorly on it.

### 5. Content Moderation Asymmetry

The same language gap that produces performance disparities in AI tools produces a specific and damaging asymmetry in how social media platforms moderate content in the Global South.

**Content moderation** — the process of identifying and removing or limiting the spread of content that violates platform policies (hate speech, incitement to violence, misinformation, harassment) — is performed partly by human reviewers and partly by automated machine learning systems. The automated systems are, like all ML systems, trained on labeled examples. The labeled examples are overwhelmingly in English.

The result is two simultaneous failures:

**Under-moderation of harmful content in non-English languages.** Because the automated systems were not trained on content in Swahili, Haitian Creole, Rohingya, or most African and Caribbean languages, they do not recognize hate speech, incitement to violence, or disinformation in those languages. Content that would be automatically flagged and removed if posted in English can circulate freely if posted in a language the system does not understand.

The consequences of this failure have been documented in several cases that resulted in real-world violence:

- **Myanmar, 2017-2018:** Facebook's failure to moderate anti-Rohingya content in Burmese contributed to the genocide against the Rohingya Muslim minority. A 2018 UN investigation cited Facebook's inadequate moderation as a contributing factor. Facebook had, at the time of the escalating violence, approximately **6 human moderators** who spoke Burmese, for a country of 54 million people.
- **Ethiopia, 2021:** Researchers documented that Facebook's systems failed to moderate content in Amharic and Oromo that was being used to coordinate violence against ethnic minorities during the Tigray conflict.
- **India:** Hate speech in Hindi, Bengali, and other Indian languages has repeatedly circulated on Facebook with minimal moderation, contributing to communal violence.

**Over-moderation of legitimate political speech.** The same inadequacy of training data that allows harmful content to pass through also causes legitimate content in non-English languages to be incorrectly flagged as harmful. Political speech, activism, journalism, and cultural expression that is entirely legitimate in its local context gets classified as violent, hateful, or dangerous by systems that cannot understand its context.

The asymmetry is exact: marginalized communities experience **more harm** because harmful content targeting them circulates freely, and **more censorship** because their own political speech gets incorrectly removed. The harm and the silencing both flow from the same failure: the platforms did not prioritize their languages when building the moderation systems.

This is not an accidental oversight. It is a resource allocation decision. Building effective moderation for a language costs money — in training data, in human reviewers, in linguistic expertise. The return on that investment is lower in markets with lower advertising revenue. The economic logic of surveillance capitalism produces under-investment in the moderation infrastructure for communities with lower purchasing power.

### 6. Data Extraction in the Global South

**WhatsApp and Meta.** WhatsApp was acquired by Facebook (now Meta) in 2014 for $19 billion — the largest technology acquisition at that time. It was acquired because of its user base: hundreds of millions of people in the Global South who use it as a primary communication tool.

As of 2024, WhatsApp has approximately **2 billion monthly active users**. The majority are in Brazil, India, Indonesia, Mexico, and Nigeria. WhatsApp is, for hundreds of millions of people in the Global South, not an optional social media platform — it is the infrastructure for communicating with employers, family members, doctors, and government services.

While WhatsApp's messages are end-to-end encrypted (meaning the content is not accessible to Meta), **metadata** is not encrypted and belongs to Meta: who you communicate with, how often, for how long, when, and from where. For 2 billion users, this metadata is an enormously valuable behavioral dataset. It maps social networks, family structures, business relationships, and daily routines at global scale.

Meta used a 2021 WhatsApp policy update to expand data-sharing between WhatsApp and Facebook's advertising infrastructure. The rollout generated significant pushback in India and Europe. In India — WhatsApp's largest market, with approximately 500 million users — the Competition Commission launched an investigation. Users were given a binary choice: accept the new terms or stop using WhatsApp. In a country where WhatsApp is primary communication infrastructure for hundreds of millions of people, "stop using WhatsApp" is not a real option.

**M-Pesa in Kenya.** M-Pesa (M for mobile, *pesa* for money in Swahili) was launched by Safaricom and Vodafone in Kenya in 2007. It is a mobile money platform that allows users to store money, transfer money, and pay for services via SMS. It has grown to serve approximately **30 million users** in Kenya alone — in a country of approximately 55 million people.

M-Pesa is extraordinary. It extended financial services to populations previously excluded from banking. It enabled economic participation for rural Kenyans with no access to bank branches. It is rightly celebrated as a case study in fintech innovation serving underserved populations.

It has also generated **30 million users' worth of comprehensive financial behavior data** — every transaction, every purchase, every transfer, every sender and recipient — for Safaricom (51% owned by the Kenyan government) and Vodafone (the technology provider). This data is among the most granular records of economic behavior in East Africa. Its use for credit scoring, insurance pricing, government analysis, and increasingly for AI training is ongoing. The users whose financial lives are encoded in this data have not been compensated for its value, and the governance frameworks governing its use are not transparent.

**The extraction irony.** The most pointed version of the data colonialism argument is this: Africa, the Caribbean, South Asia, and Latin America are generating behavioral data that trains AI systems. Those AI systems are then licensed back to African, Caribbean, South Asian, and Latin American governments and businesses — as products, at commercial prices, creating technological dependency on the very companies that extracted the training data. The resource flows out. The products flow back in. The value accumulates elsewhere.

This structure is identical to the colonial commodity export economy: raw materials extracted from the periphery, processed at the center into manufactured goods, sold back to the periphery at a markup. The names of the commodities are different. Sugar has become behavioral data. The factory has become a server farm. The markup takes the form of software licensing fees and subscription revenue.

### 7. The Data Sovereignty Movement

**Data sovereignty** is the principle that communities — whether defined by geography, ethnicity, or identity — have the right to govern the collection, use, and sharing of data generated about them.

The movement is clearest and most developed among **indigenous communities** who have, for centuries, had data about them collected, controlled, and used by others — census data, health data, land survey data, anthropological records — in ways that have repeatedly been used against their interests.

In **Canada**, the First Nations Information Governance Centre has developed the **OCAP Principles** — **Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession** — asserting that First Nations communities own data about their members and have the right to control how that data is collected, used, and shared.

In **New Zealand**, the Maori data sovereignty movement has argued that data about Maori people should be subject to Maori governance, and that AI systems trained on data about Maori people without consent should not be deployed in Maori communities.

In **Hawaii**, Native Hawaiian advocates have raised concerns about genomic data collected from Native Hawaiian populations for research — arguing that consent frameworks designed for individualized medical research are inadequate when the research affects an entire community's genetic heritage.

In 2020, a group of indigenous data scholars published the **CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance**, designed to complement the earlier FAIR data principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable):

| CARE Principle | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Collective Benefit** | Data ecosystems shall be designed and function in ways that enable indigenous peoples to derive benefit from the data |
| **Authority to Control** | Indigenous peoples' rights and interests in indigenous data must be recognized and their authority to control such data be empowered |
| **Responsibility** | Those working with indigenous data have a responsibility to share how those data are used to support indigenous peoples' self-determination and collective benefit |
| **Ethics** | Indigenous peoples' rights and wellbeing should be the primary concern at all stages of the data life cycle and across the data ecosystem |

These principles are increasingly being referenced by data governance advocates outside indigenous contexts — including Caribbean data sovereignty advocates who argue that the same logic applies to Caribbean communities whose data is extracted by global platforms with no governance role for the communities themselves.

### 8. The GDPR — What It Does and What It Doesn't

The **EU's General Data Protection Regulation**, implemented May 25, 2018, is the most significant data protection framework in the world. It applies to any organization that processes data about EU residents, regardless of where the organization is headquartered.

**What the GDPR does:**

- Requires **lawful basis** for processing personal data (consent, legitimate interest, contractual necessity, legal obligation)
- Requires **consent** to be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous — "I agree" buried in Terms of Service does not constitute valid consent under GDPR
- Grants individuals the **right to erasure** ("right to be forgotten") — you can request that a company delete your data
- Grants individuals the **right to data portability** — you can request a copy of your data in a machine-readable format
- Grants individuals the **right to object** to automated decision-making that significantly affects them
- Requires **data protection by design** — companies must build data protection into their products from the start, not bolt it on as a feature
- Establishes **data protection authorities** in each EU member state with the power to investigate, fine, and order companies to comply
- Sets fines of up to **€20 million or 4% of global annual turnover**, whichever is higher

**What the GDPR does not do:**

- It does not abolish surveillance capitalism. It requires consent, but consent theater — the cookie banners and "Accept All" buttons that most users click without reading — technically satisfies the consent requirement while preserving the underlying data collection
- It does not apply outside the EU. GDPR does not protect a Jamaican user, a Ghanaian user, or a Brazilian user from the same data extraction practices that EU law restricts
- It does not prevent the aggregation of consented data into behavioral profiles. If you consent to location tracking and you consent to social media monitoring and you consent to purchase data collection — all in separate "I agree" moments — GDPR does not prevent the company from aggregating all three into a comprehensive behavioral profile
- It has not materially reduced the revenue of surveillance capitalism companies. Meta's EU revenue has continued to grow after GDPR

The GDPR is the most rigorous data protection framework in existence and it is insufficient. This is not an argument against it. It is an argument about the scale of the problem.

### 9. The Caribbean Digital Economy

Caribbean governments and development institutions have, since approximately 2015, pursued a **Caribbean Digital Economy** agenda — seeking to attract data center investment, expand business process outsourcing (BPO), develop digital financial services, and position Caribbean territories as nodes in the global digital economy.

This agenda is partly compelling and contains real opportunities. The BPO sector employs tens of thousands of Caribbean workers. Digital financial services have extended access to unbanked populations. Caribbean tech talent is increasingly in demand globally.

But there is a structural problem that development plans often obscure.

**Data centers extract more than they contribute.** A data center in Jamaica processes data generated elsewhere, on behalf of companies headquartered elsewhere, for customers who may be anywhere. The value created — the insight, the AI model improvement, the advertising revenue — flows to the company that owns the data. What remains in Jamaica is electricity consumption, a modest number of technical employment positions, and property tax revenue. The pattern is the same as colonial export processing zones: the value-added work happens at the center; the periphery contributes infrastructure and low-cost inputs.

**BPO work in the AI era.** Much Caribbean BPO work is increasingly oriented toward AI-related tasks — data labeling, content moderation, training data annotation. This is the work that feeds the AI systems that are then deployed globally. The workers doing this work are frequently paid at the bottom of the global wage scale for this type of work (see AIS-10 on ghost labor). The connection between Caribbean BPO workers annotating training data and the global AI companies that own the resulting AI models is the same extraction relationship as everywhere else in the data economy — Caribbean labor contributes to AI value; Caribbean workers do not own equity in that value.

**The absence of data protection frameworks** means that as Caribbean territories integrate into the global digital economy, they do so without the legal infrastructure to protect either individuals or the collective value of the data generated in their territories. Without data protection law, there is no basis on which to assert that the data generated by Caribbean users should remain subject to Caribbean governance. Without data protection law, there is no mechanism to require that AI systems deployed in the Caribbean were trained or tested on Caribbean-relevant data. Without data protection law, the extraction relationship is legally unconstrained.

**What Caribbean data sovereignty would look like:**

- National data protection legislation with real enforcement capacity — not just a law on the books, but a funded regulatory authority with teeth
- Mandatory data localization requirements for certain categories of sensitive data — health, financial, biometric
- Requirements that AI systems deployed in Caribbean jurisdictions meet minimum performance standards for Caribbean languages and contexts
- A Caribbean AI Institute — a regional body that builds AI capacity, trains models on Caribbean data for Caribbean purposes, and provides technical standards for public procurement of AI systems
- Revenue sharing mechanisms — frameworks under which data platforms that derive value from Caribbean users pay into a national data dividend or public fund

None of these exist at scale. Some are being discussed. The technology is already deployed. The discussion is already years behind.

### 10. The Mine

There is a question this lesson has been building to. It is not rhetorical.

If your behavioral data is a resource — which it demonstrably is, because hundreds of billions of dollars are generated annually from it — and if resources extracted from a territory without compensation to the people who generated them is a colonial arrangement — which it structurally is — then who owns the mine?

Not the data. The mine. The infrastructure, the extraction mechanism, the processing facility, the market — the entire apparatus that converts your attention, your location, your social graph, your emotional responses, and your daily behavior into a commodity.

The current answer is: five companies. Alphabet (Google), Meta, Amazon, Apple, and Microsoft — plus ByteDance for TikTok, Tencent for WeChat, and a small number of Chinese-market equivalents. These companies own the mine. They process what comes out of it. They sell the products made from it. They determine the terms on which anyone else can access it.

You are the ore.

The political question is: does this arrangement have to be permanent? Colonial extraction was also structured to seem permanent — the legal frameworks, economic dependencies, and political arrangements of colonialism were designed to be self-reinforcing. They were ended, at enormous cost and over long struggle, because the people being extracted from organized to change the terms.

Data colonialism is more recent and the organization is less advanced. But the analysis is the same. The tools exist: data protection law, data sovereignty frameworks, public alternatives, antitrust enforcement, taxation of data extraction, community data trusts, compensated data contribution. These are not utopian proposals. They are policy instruments that have been implemented in partial forms in various jurisdictions.

The question is whether the political will to implement them exists — and whether students who understand this analysis will, when they hold power, choose to use it differently than those who came before them.

### 11. Discussion Questions

1. **The consent question:** You are 15 years old. Your school uses Google Classroom and requires you to have a Google account. You have effectively consented to Google's data collection to attend school. Is this meaningful consent? What would meaningful consent actually require, given that the service is not optional? Who should be responsible for ensuring that students' data is protected when the school requires them to use corporate platforms?

2. **The WhatsApp question:** Imagine you are a Barbadian worker who uses WhatsApp to communicate with your employer, your bank, your doctor, and your family. WhatsApp announces a new privacy policy that shares more of your metadata with Meta's advertising infrastructure. Your choices are: accept the new terms, or stop using WhatsApp and lose contact with your employer, bank, doctor, and family. Is this a choice? What would a government need to do to make this a real choice rather than a coerced compliance?

3. **The language question:** An AI-powered educational platform is being offered to Caribbean governments for use in schools. It performs very well in English and Spanish. It has not been tested in Haitian Creole or Jamaican Patois. The company says it will improve performance in those languages "over time." The government has limited resources for technology procurement. Should the government accept the platform? What conditions, if any, should it require? What does it mean for the students in Haitian Creole or Patois-speaking households if the government accepts the English-only version?

4. **The extraction model question:** Couldry and Mejias argue that data colonialism follows the same structural logic as historical colonialism. A skeptic might respond: colonialism was enforced with violence; data collection is voluntary; the comparison is exaggerated. Make the strongest version of both arguments. What evidence would determine which argument is more accurate? Does the voluntary nature of data sharing change the moral analysis?

5. **The mine question:** If you could design a system in which the behavioral data generated by Caribbean users created value that remained in the Caribbean — rather than flowing to Silicon Valley — what would that system look like? Who would own it? How would it be governed? How would it handle the tension between collective benefit and individual privacy? What would prevent a Caribbean government-owned data infrastructure from becoming its own form of surveillance?
