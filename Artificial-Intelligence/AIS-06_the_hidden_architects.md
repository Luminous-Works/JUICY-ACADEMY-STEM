### Opening Statement

> *"I changed my own odds."*  
> — Dorothy Vaughan

You have now learned how machines compute, how industry built the infrastructure, and how neural networks learn. Before we go further, we stop.

Because the story you have been told about computing — if you have been told any story at all — is missing half of its people.

The programmers who made the first computers work were women. The mathematician who trained herself in a new programming language to save her team's jobs was Black. The human computers who calculated the orbital paths that sent men to the moon were Black women.

They were not credited. They were not invited to the celebration dinners. Their names were not in the press releases. Their photographs were not in the textbooks.

This lesson is the correction.

---

### 1. Human Computers

Before electronic computers, a "computer" was a job title.

Computers — human ones — were people who performed calculations by hand. They followed algorithms written on paper, checked their results against colleagues, and filled thick ledgers with numbers that engineers and scientists depended on.

During World War II, the United States Army employed thousands of human computers to calculate **ballistic firing tables** — the charts artillerymen used to aim their guns at different distances, elevations, and atmospheric conditions. Each table required hundreds of thousands of calculations. Each calculation took a human computer minutes. The army could not produce them fast enough.

**Proposal:** Build a machine that could compute faster than humans.

The machine was called **ENIAC** — Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer. It was built at the University of Pennsylvania, funded by the Army Ballistic Research Laboratory. It came online in 1945.

ENIAC was the hardware. It was not, by itself, capable of doing anything useful.

It needed programmers.

### 2. The ENIAC Six

The Army chose six women to program ENIAC. They were:

**Kay McNulty** (Kathleen Rita McNulty Mauchly Antonelli)  
Born in County Donegal, Ireland. Had studied mathematics at Chestnut Hill College. Selected as a human computer for the Army in 1942.

**Betty Jennings** (Jean Jennings Bartik)  
Born in Missouri. Had studied mathematics at Northwest Missouri State Teachers College. Described as the best programmer on the team by colleagues.

**Betty Snyder** (Frances Elizabeth "Betty" Snyder Holberton)  
Born in Philadelphia. Later invented the first software application, the sort-merge generator for UNIVAC. Worked on COBOL and FORTRAN.

**Marlyn Wescoff** (Marlyn Wescoff Meltzer)  
Born in Philadelphia. One of the original six programmers.

**Fran Bilas** (Frances Bilas Spence)  
Born in Philadelphia. Programmed ENIAC alongside her sister-in-law Ruth.

**Ruth Lichterman** (Ruth Lichterman Teitelbaum)  
Born in New York City. Among the first to teach ENIAC programming to others.

These six women were given no manuals. There were none. They were given the machine's blueprints — hundreds of pages of electrical diagrams — and told to figure it out.

They did. They learned to program ENIAC by studying its physical switches, cables, and panels directly. They invented the concept of subroutines. They debugged the hardware when it misbehaved, because there was no distinction yet between hardware and software problems.

**The ENIAC's public debut was February 1, 1946.**

The six women were not introduced at the event. Photographs from the day show them standing near the machine — they were assumed to be models posed for press photographs.

Their names were not in the press release.

It took fifty years before their work was documented.

The effort to recover their history was led by journalist and historian **Kathy Kleiman**, whose 2022 documentary *The Computers* is the definitive record.

### 3. Dorothy Vaughan — The First Black Supervisor at NACA

**Dorothy Johnson Vaughan.** Born September 20, 1910, in Kansas City, Missouri.

She was a mathematics teacher at Robert Russa Moton High School in Farmville, Virginia — a segregated school for Black students. In 1943, she was recruited to work as a "computer" at the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA), the precursor to NASA, at Langley Research Center in Virginia.

Under Jim Crow law, Black employees at Langley were assigned to a separate section: the **West Computing** group. They worked in a segregated building. They used separate bathrooms, a separate cafeteria, a separate library.

They were also among the most capable mathematicians on site.

**In 1949, Dorothy Vaughan became the first Black supervisor at NACA.** She managed the West Computing group — a team of Black women mathematicians who performed aeronautical calculations for engineers across the entire research center.

In the late 1950s, NACA transitioned to electronic computers — IBM machines that threatened to replace the human computing teams. Vaughan recognized the threat. She went to the Langley library, found a book on **FORTRAN** — the programming language IBM used — checked it out, and taught herself.

She then taught FORTRAN to every member of her team.

When the IBM computers came online, Vaughan's team were the only people at Langley who knew how to program them. Instead of being replaced, they became the programmers.

Dorothy Vaughan's career at NASA continued until 1971. She spent 28 years contributing to research that shaped modern aviation and spaceflight.

She died on November 10, 2008 — eight years before she appeared in the film *Hidden Figures*.

### 4. Katherine Johnson — The Orbital Mechanic

**Katherine Coleman Goble Johnson.** Born August 26, 1918, in White Sulphur Springs, West Virginia.

She entered college at 18, graduated at 18 with degrees in mathematics and French, and began a teaching career in West Virginia. In 1953, she was hired by NACA's West Area Computing unit.

Her work:
- Calculated the trajectory for **Alan Shepard's Freedom 7** — the first American crewed spaceflight (May 5, 1961)
- Calculated the trajectory for **John Glenn's Friendship 7** — the first American orbital spaceflight (February 20, 1962)

For the Glenn mission, the flight controllers used IBM computers to calculate the orbital parameters. Glenn refused to fly unless Katherine Johnson personally verified the numbers by hand.

She did. The mission launched. It succeeded.

Her calculation of the trans-lunar injection trajectory and rendezvous for **Apollo 11** contributed directly to the first lunar landing.

Katherine Johnson received the **Presidential Medal of Freedom** in 2015. A building at NASA Langley was named for her in 2016. She died on February 24, 2020, at the age of 101.

---

### 5. Mary Jackson — NASA's First Black Female Engineer

**Mary Winston Jackson.** Born April 9, 1921, in Hampton, Virginia.

She joined NACA's segregated West Computing group in 1951. In 1958, she became **NASA's first Black female engineer** after completing additional coursework at Hampton High School — a white-only school she had to petition the city of Hampton for permission to attend.

Her engineering work focused on wind tunnel research — the aerodynamics of aircraft and spacecraft. She worked on the forces that govern how air flows around structures at high speeds.

She retired from engineering in 1985 and spent the rest of her career managing NASA's Equal Opportunity Specialist programs, working to improve conditions for women and minorities in the workforce she had navigated for 35 years.

A building at NASA headquarters in Washington, D.C. is now named the **Mary W. Jackson NASA Headquarters Building**.

### 6. The Pattern

These are not isolated examples. They are a pattern.

**Ada Lovelace (1815–1852):** Wrote the first algorithm intended to be processed by a machine — for Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine. She is now recognized as the first computer programmer. For most of the 20th century, she was a footnote.

**Grace Hopper (1906–1992):** Developed the first compiler (A-0) — software that translates human-readable code into machine code. She led the team that developed COBOL. She held the rank of Rear Admiral in the US Navy. Her contributions to computing infrastructure are foundational. She was chronically undercited in her lifetime.

**Hedy Lamarr (1914–2000):** Film actress and inventor. Co-invented frequency-hopping spread spectrum — the technology that underlies WiFi, Bluetooth, and GPS. Her patent was appropriated by the US military and expired before she could profit from it.

**The ENIAC Six:** Named above. Assumed to be models in photographs.

**Dorothy Vaughan:** Segregated, uncredited, essential.

**Joan Clarke (1917–1996):** Alan Turing's cryptanalyst colleague at Bletchley Park. Led the Banburismus section that broke the Naval Enigma. Named in Turing's private correspondence as one of his closest friends. Missing from most histories of Bletchley.

The technology you use every day was built substantially by people who were not invited to the table.

### 7. Why This Was Allowed to Happen

This was not accidental.

The erasure of women from computing history followed a specific economic logic:

In the early computer industry, programming was considered **clerical work** — analogous to typing. It was therefore assigned to women (who were given clerical work). It was underpaid. It was considered low-status.

As the economic and strategic value of software became clear — through the Cold War, aerospace, and commerce — the field became male-dominated. The rebranding of programming as highly skilled, technical, and prestigious work coincided with the exclusion of women.

The women who had built the field were reclassified as support staff, or simply not mentioned.

**The same logic applied to Black scientists:**
- West Computing existed because Black mathematicians could not work alongside white mathematicians, so their work was segregated — and therefore invisible in the official record.
- Their names did not appear in the research papers they contributed to.
- Dorothy Vaughan's name is not on any of the aeronautical research she supported.

This is not ancient history. It describes how knowledge is produced, attributed, and controlled — and who benefits.

### 8. The Caribbean Thread

In this curriculum, we have already met:
- **Dr. Cicely Williams** (WIS-06) — Jamaican physician who identified kwashiorkor. Her paper was ignored for years. A male colleague was later credited.
- **Dr. Mercedes Richards** (WIS-07) — Jamaican astrophysicist. The first Black woman to receive tenure at a major US research university in astrophysics. Built computational tools for stellar analysis from scratch.
- **Dr. Valrie Grant** (WIS-09) — Jamaican remote sensing scientist. Applied satellite data to Jamaican land management. Her work gives planners visibility into deforestation, flooding, and agricultural change.

The erasure is not exclusive to the United States. It operates wherever credit flows upward through hierarchies of race and gender.

The question for you — the student in this classroom, in this Caribbean — is not whether this happened. It happened. The question is what you do with that knowledge as you build the next generation of systems.

### Review Questions

1. The ENIAC Six learned to program the machine without a manual — by reading the physical blueprints. What does this tell us about the nature of programming expertise?

2. When John Glenn asked Katherine Johnson to verify the IBM computers' orbital calculations, what was he actually saying about trust in automated systems? Is this relevant to AI today?

3. Dorothy Vaughan taught herself FORTRAN from a library book and trained her entire team. What does this demonstrate about how skills can be distributed within a community?

4. Why was programming considered low-status work in the 1940s, and how did its status change? What drove that change?

5. Name one person from Caribbean history whose scientific contribution you believe has been underrecognized. What would it mean to formally document their work?

### Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Human computer | A person employed to perform mathematical calculations, before electronic computers |
| ENIAC | Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer; the first general-purpose electronic computer, 1945 |
| West Computing | The segregated division of Black women mathematicians at NACA/NASA Langley |
| FORTRAN | Formula Translation; one of the first high-level programming languages, developed in the 1950s |
| COBOL | Common Business-Oriented Language; programming language for business applications |
| Subroutine | A reusable section of code that can be called from multiple places in a program |
| Attribution | The recognition of who created, discovered, or contributed to a piece of work |
